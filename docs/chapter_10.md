# Chapter 10: Ultrasound-Guided FNA and Core Biopsy

---

## Why This Chapter Matters

Across the three bread-and-butter workflows of UK head and neck ultrasound — two-week-wait (2WW) GP referrals, head and neck oncology MDTs, and outpatient ENT clinics — tissue sampling under ultrasound guidance is a daily expectation. The technical act of passing a needle into a target is the easy part; the harder skill is deciding *which* lesion to sample, *with what* technique, *for which* question, and *how* to ensure the sample is handled so that it actually answers that question.

The organising principle for everything in this chapter is: **the right lesion, sampled in the right way, with the right handling, for the right clinical question.** Every decision below flows from that.

## Indications and Clinical Pathways

### When Tissue Sampling Changes Management

Tissue sampling is warranted when imaging alone cannot resolve the clinical question *and* the result will influence what happens next. The commonest indications in UK practice are:

- Characterisation of a neck mass where ultrasound features are indeterminate or suspicious
- Nodal staging in known head and neck cancer when the result would alter stage or treatment intent
- Investigation of persistent cervical lymphadenopathy in adults (especially 2WW referrals)
- Thyroid nodule assessment guided by the BTA U1–U5 scoring system
- Salivary gland masses requiring preoperative diagnosis
- Suspected recurrence in the treated neck

Ask yourself before every procedure: *what single result would most help the MDT decide the next step?* If you cannot answer that, clarify the clinical question first.

### The 2WW Neck Lump Pathway

Most 2WW "neck lump >3 weeks" referrals can be triaged at the ultrasound examination itself. The practical three-way decision is:

- **Benign or reactive** (e.g., reactive lymphadenopathy, simple ranula, Warthin's tumour with classic features): reassure, discharge, or refer back to GP with explanation
- **Indeterminate** (e.g., atypical node, solid salivary mass without diagnostic features): tissue sampling, often on the same visit if prepared
- **Suspicious or malignant** (e.g., node with loss of hilum, necrosis, cystic change in a middle-aged adult): sample immediately and fast-track to MDT

A one-stop approach — scan and sample on the same visit — is efficient and patient-friendly provided the pots, forms, and laboratory requests are set up in advance. It requires preparation, not improvisation.

### The U-Score and Biopsy Thresholds for Thyroid Nodules

The BTA U1–U5 system stratifies ultrasound risk and guides biopsy decisions. In broad terms:

- **U1 (normal)** and **U2 (benign)**: no biopsy in the absence of discordant clinical concern
- **U3 (indeterminate/equivocal)**: biopsy threshold depends on nodule size and local risk factors — thresholds vary between centres and should follow local protocol
- **U4 (suspicious)** and **U5 (malignant)**: biopsy is recommended

Do not apply these thresholds mechanically. A U3 nodule in a patient with a history of neck irradiation or a family history of thyroid cancer warrants a lower threshold than the same nodule in a low-risk individual. Use the score as a framework for communication, not as a substitute for clinical judgement.

### Cervical Node Levels: Language That Matters

Always use the standardised level I–VI nomenclature (with sublevels where appropriate) when describing and labelling nodal specimens. This orients the surgeon and oncologist immediately: a level II cystic node has a different differential to a level IV solid node. Report the **level, side, and dominant sonographic features** every time.

## Choosing Between FNA and Core Biopsy

This is a decision driven by pathology, not habit.

### Fine Needle Aspiration

FNA (21–25G) provides cytology — individual cells and small clusters — without architectural context. It is quick, well tolerated, compressible, and carries negligible seeding risk. It works well when the diagnosis can be made from cellular morphology alone:

- Metastatic squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) in cervical nodes
- Thyroid lesions (reported using the RCPath Thy1–Thy5 system, conceptually aligned with the Bethesda classification)
- Classic salivary lesions: Warthin's tumour, pleomorphic adenoma
- Cystic neck masses being triaged for HPV/EBV studies

FNA struggles when architectural pattern is diagnostically essential — most notably lymphoma — or when the lesion is fibrous, necrotic, or in a previously treated and scarred field.

### Core Needle Biopsy

Core biopsy (typically 18G) provides histological cores with preserved architecture, enabling immunohistochemistry and reliably supporting a lymphoma subtype. It is preferred when:

- Lymphoma is suspected and architectural subtyping is required
- FNA has been non-diagnostic or Thy3/indeterminate on more than one occasion
- A salivary lesion is atypical or cytologically suspicious for malignancy and histological confirmation will guide surgical planning
- The neck has been previously treated and fibrosis has rendered cytology uninterpretable

Core biopsy carries a slightly higher bleeding risk and demands more precise technique near vessels and nerves. For salivary gland work-ups, many UK centres use FNA first and reserve core for non-diagnostic or ambiguous results — this is a reasonable approach and practice does vary.

A few situations call for **neither**: do not biopsy percutaneously a lesion that looks like a paraganglioma. The hypervascular mass at the carotid bifurcation with splaying of internal and external carotid arteries should be diagnosed on cross-sectional imaging and biochemistry. Vascular malformations and lesions with high arteriovenous flow should similarly be approached with caution; discuss with the appropriate specialist before proceeding.

## Pre-Procedure Planning

### Review Before You Scan

Never approach a sampling procedure cold. Before positioning the patient, review the clinical question, any prior imaging (including CT, MRI, or PET-CT), and the previous ultrasound findings if available. Identify:

- The most diagnostically useful target (the most suspicious node at the most accessible level; the solid component of a mixed nodule)
- Viable tissue within that target (avoid necrosis and cysts alone)
- The vascular anatomy on Doppler, with a planned safe corridor
- The proximity of carotid and jugular vessels, the facial artery, retromandibular vein, and airway

Decide at this stage which ancillary tests you will need, and confirm that the correct containers, transport media, and laboratory request forms are to hand before a single needle passes through the skin. A technically perfect sample sent in the wrong pot — or without a flow cytometry request form — is a wasted opportunity.

### Anticoagulation

Head and neck sampling is generally compressible and carries a low haemorrhage risk. That said, follow your local policy and British Society of Interventional Radiology (BSIR) guidance for individual agents. Broadly:

- **Aspirin**: usually continued
- **P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor)**: consider holding 5–7 days if clinically safe, with specialist input
- **Warfarin**: target INR ≤1.5
- **Direct oral anticoagulants**: typically omit 24–48 hours pre-procedure based on renal function and bleeding risk; resume once haemostasis is confirmed

### Consent

Consent should be specific to the actual procedure, not generic. Discuss: expected benefit (tissue diagnosis to guide management), likely complications (pain, bruising, haematoma), uncommon risks (infection, transient neuropraxia), rare risks (inadequate sample requiring repeat procedure, extremely rare tumour seeding), and alternatives (surveillance imaging, surgical biopsy). Document this clearly.

## Equipment and Setup

- **Probe**: high-frequency linear (12–18 MHz) for superficial targets; a lower-frequency linear (5–9 MHz) for deep targets or large necks. Use the highest frequency that gives adequate penetration.
- **Needles**: 22–23G is a practical all-rounder for FNA; 25G for delicate thyroid work or highly vascular lesions; 18G automated spring-loaded device for core biopsy, with a 10–15 mm throw as a versatile starting point. Use echogenic needles where available.
- **Doppler**: optimise for low-flow detection before every procedure — lower the pulse repetition frequency, increase sensitivity, and sweep the target in two planes to understand the three-dimensional vascular layout.
- **Ancillaries**: sterile probe cover, sterile gel, chlorhexidine or iodine prep, lidocaine 1%, syringes, gauze, specimen containers labelled in advance.

## Technique Fundamentals

### Patient Positioning

The goal is a relaxed patient, a clear path to the target, and an operator who can maintain eye–hand–image alignment without twisting awkwardly.

- **Thyroid and anterior neck**: supine, shoulders elevated on a pillow, neck gently extended, head turned away from the side of interest
- **Parotid**: supine, head turned contralaterally, jaw relaxed; for the parotid tail and level II nodes, a slight sniffing position opens up the submandibular space
- **Posterior triangle (level V)**: supine with a shoulder roll, or occasionally seated

### Needle Visualisation: The Physical Principle

Needles are reflectors of ultrasound, not inherent generators of signal. They appear bright when the ultrasound beam strikes them close to perpendicular — and dim or invisible when the angle is shallow. This is why needles at steep angles in superficial tissue are easy to see, while a shallow approach to a deep target can render the needle nearly invisible.

Practical consequences:

- Use an **in-plane approach** whenever possible: the entire shaft and tip are visible in a single image plane, and you always know where the tip is
- **Heel–toe the probe** to make the beam more perpendicular to the needle shaft if visibility is poor
- Use **beam steering and needle enhancement modes** if your machine has them
- **Out-of-plane approaches** work well for tight anatomical spaces but demand strict tip control — advance only 1–2 mm at a time, constantly re-centre the point of maximum brightness, and never fire a core device until you can see the tip confidently within the target
- **If you cannot see the needle tip, stop and re-optimise** before advancing further. This is non-negotiable.

### Hydrodissection

A small volume (1–3 ml) of saline or dilute lidocaine can be injected along the planned tract to push a vessel or nerve away from the needle path. This is particularly useful in the parotid and at level II where the facial vessels or internal jugular vein may be inconveniently positioned. It is a simple, underused technique.

### Local Anaesthetic

Infiltrate 1–2 ml of 1% lidocaine along the tract to the lesion surface. Do not infiltrate *into* the lesion itself — excess fluid dilutes cytology and can create artefact. Adequate local anaesthesia improves patient cooperation and reduces movement artefact.

### FNA Technique

- **Capillary (non-aspiration) technique**: insert the needle without a syringe attached and move briskly back and forth within the target using capillary action to fill the hub. This is ideal for vascular or friable lesions where suction would introduce blood and dilute the sample.
- **Aspiration technique**: apply 2–5 ml of gentle suction during passes for fibrous or less cellular lesions. Release suction before withdrawing to prevent the sample being aspirated into the syringe barrel.
- Make 2–3 passes directed at different viable areas of the same target. Target the **solid, hypoechoic, irregular components** — avoid pure necrosis, pure cyst fluid (unless it is what you specifically need), and the fatty hilum of nodes.

### Core Biopsy Technique

- Position the closed needle tip at the near edge of the target with the throw length calculated to sit the sample trough entirely within viable tissue.
- Confirm needle position, confirm Doppler corridor is safe, then fire.
- One to three cores usually suffice for most lesions; for lymphoma, aim for three or more and coordinate with haematopathology in advance regarding sample handling.
- Apply firm compression for at least five minutes after core biopsy; re-scan to exclude haematoma before the patient leaves.

## Procedure-Specific Guidance

### Thyroid

Apply the U-score to determine whether FNA is indicated. When it is:

- Target the solid, hypoechoic, taller-than-wide, microcalcified, or extrathyroidally extending component — in other words, the features driving the U4 or U5 designation
- In mixed cystic–solid nodules, aspirate the cyst contents first to deflate the nodule, then sample the mural nodule or solid septa directly
- 23–25G capillary technique for most nodules; 2–3 passes
- For a suspicious lateral neck node in a patient with a thyroid lesion, request **thyroglobulin washout** from the FNA needle: rinse the needle in 1 ml of saline and send for thyroglobulin measurement — a markedly elevated value strongly supports metastatic thyroid carcinoma
- **PTH washout** can confirm parathyroid tissue when the imaging differential is uncertain. However, avoid routine percutaneous sampling of parathyroid lesions as it can cause fibrosis and complicate subsequent surgery; reserve this for genuine diagnostic dilemmas
- Core biopsy is appropriate after two non-diagnostic FNA attempts in a solid nodule, or when a non-thyroidal process (e.g., lymphoma in the context of Hashimoto's disease) is being considered

Report using RCPath Thy1–Thy5. Thy1 (non-diagnostic) should trigger a repeat FNA with careful attention to targeting solid areas; two Thy1 results from a solid nodule warrant discussion about core biopsy or surgical excision.

### Cervical Lymph Nodes

Select the **most suspicious accessible node** at the most relevant level. Suspicious features that should guide your choice include: focal cortical thickening or bulging, loss of the fatty hilum, round morphology, necrosis, microcalcifications, and peripheral or chaotic vascularity on Doppler. One well-targeted pass from a classic malignant node is more valuable than multiple mediocre passes from a borderline one.

Target the **cortex**, not the hilum. In a necrotic node, use Doppler to find the viable rim and sample there — the necrotic centre will yield only debris.

**Cystic level II nodes in a middle-aged adult** should be treated as metastatic SCC from an HPV-positive oropharyngeal primary until proved otherwise. Do not reassure the patient that "it looks like a branchial cyst." Aspirate fluid for cytology and request **HPV DNA PCR** and/or **p16 immunocytochemistry**; even a small cluster of atypical squamous cells in fluid that returns HPV-positive is diagnostic. Also target any mural nodule sonographically.

**Suspected lymphoma**: core biopsy is strongly preferred because architectural subtyping determines treatment. If core is not feasible, perform multiple FNA passes and dedicate some specifically to flow cytometry — rinse the needle in appropriate transport medium (confirm with your haematopathology laboratory before you start). Flow cytometry adds substantially to FNA yield in lymphoma.

**Suspected TB or atypical mycobacterial infection**: send material in a sterile pot or saline for AFB smear, culture, and PCR. Do not put it in formalin. This is a common and costly error.

**Level distribution as a differential aid**: a cystic level II node in an adult points to HPV-related oropharyngeal SCC; level II–III nodes with EBV positivity point to nasopharyngeal carcinoma; level V nodes suggest a cutaneous primary or lymphoma; bilateral level VI nodes suggest thyroid carcinoma or lymphoma.

### Salivary Glands

FNA is first-line for most parotid and submandibular masses. It reliably distinguishes benign from malignant in many cases, and the cytological pattern of Warthin's tumour (lymphocytes in a granular background) or pleomorphic adenoma (fibromyxoid stroma with epithelial cells) can be diagnostic.

When sampling the parotid, stay lateral to the retromandibular vein where possible to minimise risk to the facial nerve plane. The nerve itself is not usually seen on ultrasound, but the retromandibular vein is a useful proxy landmark.
