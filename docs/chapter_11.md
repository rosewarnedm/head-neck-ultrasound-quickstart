# Chapter 11: Other Soft-Tissue and Miscellaneous Neck Pathology

---

## Why this chapter matters

Most head and neck ultrasound lists in UK practice are a mixture: GP "neck lump" referrals, ENT clinic patients, and oncology surveillance. A significant proportion of these lumps lie outside the thyroid, salivary glands, and lymph nodes. Without a principled approach to "everything else," it is easy to miss a malignancy, biopsy something you should not, or fail to direct the patient into the correct pathway — two-week-wait suspected cancer, sarcoma MDT, or a vascular anomalies service.

This chapter teaches you how to reason through these lesions systematically: understanding why they look the way they do, how to examine them, when and how to sample them, and when to escalate.

---

## How to approach a non-glandular neck lump

### Triage and clinical context

Before you apply gel, synthesise the referral. Age is your first discriminator:

- **Children and young adults**: congenital and developmental lesions predominate — branchial cleft cysts, thyroglossal duct cysts, lymphatic malformations, infantile haemangiomas.
- **Adults over 40, smokers, or those with an alcohol history**: metastatic nodal disease, skin SCC metastases, and lymphoma must sit at the top of your differential. Any unexplained persistent neck mass beyond three weeks in an adult should be treated as malignant until proved otherwise and triaged on the two-week-wait pathway.
- **Any age with rapid growth, pain out of proportion to appearance, or fever**: consider abscess, necrotising fasciitis, or an aggressive tumour.

A few red flags demand a specific response before you even start scanning:

| Red flag | Action |
|---|---|
| Cystic lateral mass in an adult | Assume cystic nodal metastasis; do not label as branchial cyst without sampling |
| Deep-to-fascia solid mass >5 cm or rapidly growing | Refer on sarcoma pathway before any biopsy |
| Fluctuation with Valsalva or positional change | Consider venous/lymphatic malformation or laryngocele |
| Airway symptoms, trismus, or dysphagia | Urgent CT; ultrasound is insufficient for deep space infection |
| Pulsatile mass with bruit/thrill | Suspect paraganglioma or AVM; do not biopsy |

Keep UK referral pathways in mind throughout: two-week-wait for suspected head and neck cancer, sarcoma MDT for suspicious soft-tissue masses, vascular anomalies MDT for malformations, and BTA U1–U5 scoring with NICE-threshold FNA if you incidentally encounter a thyroid nodule.

### Ultrasound technique and systematic sweep

#### Probe selection and machine settings

A high-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz) is appropriate for most superficial neck structures. Drop to 6–9 MHz for deep or bulky necks. A small footprint ("hockey-stick") probe aids access in the submandibular space, paediatric patients, and tight anatomical recesses. For very superficial cutaneous lesions, use a standoff pad or generous gel pool to bring the lesion into the focal zone.

On the machine:
- **Grey scale**: place focal zones at and just below the lesion. Compound imaging reduces speckle but can smooth out small calcifications — be aware of this trade-off.
- **Colour Doppler**: start with a low pulse repetition frequency (0.5–1 kHz) and low wall filter to detect slow venous flow. Increase PRF for high-flow lesions to avoid aliasing. Use spectral Doppler to characterise waveforms (low-resistance arterial flow suggests paraganglioma or haemangioma; venous augmentation with compression suggests venous malformation).
- **Microvascular or power Doppler imaging**, where available, improves detection of low-volume flow in parathyroid adenomas and small tumours.

#### Dynamic manoeuvres

Do not scan in a static fashion. Dynamic assessment is often diagnostic:
- **Swallowing and tongue protrusion**: thyroglossal duct cysts ascend; midline strap muscle lesions move differently; this single manoeuvre can confirm or exclude a TGDC.
- **Valsalva and dependency**: venous and lymphatic malformations visibly distend; laryngoceles enlarge with phonation or Valsalva.
- **Gentle compression**: venous and lymphatic malformations are compressible; solid tumours and abscesses are not. Compressibility also helps differentiate a normal vein from a pseudomass.

#### Systematic sweep and documentation

Organise your examination by compartment and fascial plane:
1. Superficial/subcutaneous layer (above platysma)
2. Platysma and subcutaneous fat
3. Intramuscular (SCM, strap muscles, scalenes)
4. Deep to investing fascia (carotid sheath, visceral space, parapharyngeal)

Map the lesion to cervical nodal levels I–VI — this immediately contextualises your report for ENT and oncology colleagues. Record: three-plane size, depth relative to fascia, margins, internal texture, posterior acoustic features, calcification or gas, vascular pattern, and behaviour with dynamic manoeuvres.

#### Thinking in terms of tissue physics

The most useful habit you can develop is asking *why* a lesion looks the way it does:
- **Fluid** attenuates sound poorly, producing posterior enhancement; simple cysts are anechoic and brightly enhanced.
- **Protein or cellular debris** increases echogenicity within fluid, reducing transmission but maintaining some enhancement.
- **Fat** is relatively echogenic with characteristic linear striations parallel to the skin.
- **Muscle** is intermediate echogenicity with parallel fibroadipose septa; normal fascicular architecture is preserved.
- **Nerve** shows a fascicular "honeycomb" pattern in short axis.
- **Calcification and gas** are strongly reflective, generating posterior shadowing; gas also produces ring-down or comet-tail artefacts.

Once you internalise these principles, you can reason from appearance to tissue composition rather than simply pattern-matching.

## Congenital and developmental lesions

### Thyroglossal duct cyst

The thyroid descends from the foramen caecum to the anterior neck during embryogenesis. Failure of this tract to obliterate leaves a residual cyst — most commonly at or just below the hyoid bone, which explains the pathognomonic sign of upward movement with swallowing and tongue protrusion.

**Sonographic appearance**: Midline or paramedian, round or oval cyst with posterior enhancement. Contents range from anechoic (simple) to echogenic with internal debris if infected or proteinaceous. A quiescent cyst has a thin, non-vascular wall; an infected cyst has a thickened, hyperaemic wall with surrounding tissue oedema.

**Critical caution**: In adults, a midline cystic lesion with a solid mural nodule or microcalcifications should prompt sampling of the solid component — papillary thyroid carcinoma can arise within a TGDC. This is rare but not negligible. Also, always confirm that normal orthotopic thyroid tissue is present; occasionally the TGDC contains the only functioning thyroid tissue, which has serious implications before any surgical referral.

**Management**: Uncomplicated TGDCs are treated with Sistrunk procedure (excision of the cyst with the central hyoid body and suprahyoid tract) after any acute infection has settled. If ectopic thyroid tissue is suspected or the patient is hypothyroid, correlate with TFTs and radionuclide scintigraphy before proceeding.

### Dermoid and epidermoid cysts

These are inclusion cysts containing keratinous material (epidermoid) or skin appendage structures including sebaceous glands and hair follicles (dermoid). Their high lipid and keratin content creates multiple internal reflectors, accounting for the characteristic heterogeneous echotexture.

**Sonographic appearance**: Both are well-circumscribed, subcutaneous masses. Dermoids tend to be more heterogeneous, sometimes with echogenic "dots and dashes" and shadowing from ossified or calcified elements. Epidermoids classically appear hypoechoic with a "pseudotestis" pattern of uniform low-level internal echoes. Neither lesion has internal vascularity on Doppler; posterior enhancement is variable depending on content. A punctum or dermal tether suggests an epidermal inclusion cyst.

**Key differentials**: Lipoma (homogeneously echogenic with linear striations), branchial cleft cyst (lateral, related to SCM), and pilomatricoma (see below).

### Branchial cleft anomalies

Second branchial cleft cysts — the most common type — lie along the anterior border of the SCM at the level of the carotid bifurcation, posterior to the submandibular gland, at level II. Sinuses or fistulae may track medially to the tonsillar fossa.

**Sonographic appearance**: Well-defined cystic mass, typically anechoic in children. Internal debris is common after infection. The wall is thin and avascular when quiescent, thickened and hyperaemic when inflamed. Fistulous tracts occasionally appear as tubular hypoechoic structures tracking towards the skin or pharynx.

**The adult trap**: In any patient over 30–40 years, a cystic mass at level II is a cystic nodal metastasis until proved otherwise. HPV-associated oropharyngeal SCC — increasingly common — typically presents exactly this way: a cystic level IIa or IIb node, sometimes with no clinically obvious primary. Features favouring metastasis include thick or irregularly thickened walls, eccentric solid mural nodules, heterogeneous internal echoes, extracapsular fat stranding, and loss of contralateral nodal symmetry. Do not drain these lesions or label them as branchial cysts without sampling.

**Action**: Perform ultrasound-guided FNA targeting the solid component or wall. Send for cytology and HPV/p16 testing. Refer on the two-week-wait pathway for urgent ENT mucosal assessment and nasoendoscopy.

### Lymphatic malformations and venolymphatic malformations

These are developmental endothelial malformations — not tumours — arising from failure of lymphatic channels to connect to the venous system. Macrocystic lesions (spaces >1–2 cm) are the classic "cystic hygroma" of the posterior triangle in neonates. Microcystic forms infiltrate tissues diffusely. Mixed venolymphatic malformations are common.

**Sonographic appearance**: Multiloculated, thin-walled cystic lesion with echogenic septa, typically transpatial and insinuating around neurovascular structures. The lesion is compressible and enlarges with Valsalva and dependency. Internal echoes indicate haemorrhage or superinfection. Phleboliths — echogenic foci with posterior shadowing and a characteristic "twinkling" artefact on colour Doppler — indicate a venous component. Arterial flow is absent or minimal; septal vascularity may be present.

**Management**: Do not biopsy. The imaging goal is defining the extent and its relationship to the airway, salivary spaces, and neurovascular bundles. Request MRI for comprehensive mapping. Refer to a vascular anomalies MDT; ultrasound guidance is used therapeutically for sclerotherapy access, not diagnostically.

### Infantile haemangiomas versus vascular malformations

This distinction matters clinically because their behaviour and management are entirely different. Use the ISSVA classification as your framework: haemangiomas are true vascular tumours with a proliferative phase followed by spontaneous involution; malformations are structural anomalies that do not involute.

**Proliferative infantile haemangioma**: Solid, lobulated, heterogeneous, with abundant low-resistance arterial and venous flow on Doppler. Often subcutaneous with skin discolouration. The vascular density distinguishes it from most other solid lesions. As it involutes, it becomes more echogenic due to fat replacement, and vascularity diminishes.

**Venous malformation**: Compressible, phleboliths present, minimal arterial flow (see later section).

**AVM**: High-flow shunts with feeding arteries and draining veins, no discrete soft-tissue mass.

Do not biopsy a suspected haemangioma. If there is diagnostic uncertainty — particularly between a proliferative haemangioma and a more aggressive vascular tumour — refer to the vascular anomalies MDT.

### Plunging ranula

A plunging ranula is a mucus extravasation pseudocyst arising from the sublingual gland. When the mucocoele herniates through or around the mylohyoid, it presents as a neck lump in the submandibular space.

**Sonographic appearance**: Well-defined, anechoic or hypoechoic cystic lesion in the submandibular space, without internal vascularity. Look for continuity with a sublingual cyst above the mylohyoid. The mylohyoid defect through which it has herniated may occasionally be visualised. There is no true epithelial lining — this is an extravasation cyst, not a retention cyst.

**Next steps**: MRI for surgical planning. Treatment targets the sublingual gland, not simply the neck component.

### Laryngocele

Ultrasound is limited for laryngoceles because air generates strong artefact. However, if the sac is fluid-filled (laryngomucocele or pyolaryngocele), it may present as a cystic neck mass adjacent to the thyrohyoid membrane that visibly enlarges with Valsalva. If you identify such a lesion, correlate with laryngoscopy and CT to define the internal versus external components and to exclude an obstructing laryngeal lesion — an important association.

## Infective and inflammatory processes

### Cellulitis, phlegmon, and abscess

These form a pathological continuum, and ultrasound is well placed to identify where along that continuum a patient lies — a distinction that directly determines whether the patient needs antibiotics, incision and drainage, or urgent CT.

**Cellulitis**: Increased tissue water manifests as subcutaneous thickening with a "cobblestone" appearance — lobules of subcutaneous fat separated by thickened, hyperechoic oedematous septa. No drainable focus.

**Phlegmon**: An ill-defined, hypoechoic area of inflamed tissue with hyperaemia on Doppler. Not yet liquefied; drainage would not be productive.

**Abscess**: Once liquefaction necrosis has occurred, a discrete collection forms: anechoic to complex fluid, often with swirling internal debris visible with gentle probe pressure, posterior enhancement, and peripheral hyperaemia without central flow on Doppler. This is the stage at which drainage is indicated.

When marking for drainage, use colour Doppler to identify the safest path, avoiding vessels and major nerves. Guide the needle into the dependent portion of the collection.

**Necrotising fasciitis** is a surgical emergency. Look for thickened hyperechoic fascia, fluid tracking along fascial planes, and — critically — echogenic foci within the tissues causing "dirty" posterior shadowing from gas. If you suspect this, escalate immediately rather than completing the ultrasound examination.

### Deep space infection

Ultrasound performs well in the submandibular, masticator, and buccal spaces, but is substantially limited for the parapharyngeal, retropharyngeal, and danger spaces due to interference from the mandible and airway. If there is any suggestion of deep space involvement, trismus, dysphagia, or systemic sepsis, the correct investigation is contrast-enhanced CT. Use your ultrasound findings to identify what you can, then make the case clearly in your report for CT when the clinical picture demands it.

### Mycobacterial infection

Tuberculous lymphadenitis produces a characteristic constellation: nodes with central caseating necrosis appearing as anechoic or heterogeneously hypoechoic centres, matted groups of nodes, sinus tracts tracking to the skin, and thick irregular walls. "Cold abscesses" have thin walls and relatively little surrounding hyperaemia compared to pyogenic abscesses — a useful differentiator. Calcifications may be present in chronic or treated disease.

**Sampling**: Ultrasound-guided FNA or core biopsy for AFB smear, culture, and mycobacterial PCR. Communicate with the cytology and microbiology teams in advance regarding infection control and specimen handling requirements.
