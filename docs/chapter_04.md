# Chapter 4: Diffuse Thyroid Disease and the Diffusely Abnormal Gland

---

## Why This Chapter Matters

Most thyroid ultrasound referrals in UK practice arrive from three sources: GP neck-lump and thyroid function clinics, ENT outpatient lists for goitre and compressive symptoms, and head and neck oncology pathways including two-week-wait (2WW) referrals where thyroid malignancy is a differential. In all three settings you must do two distinct jobs simultaneously — characterise the background parenchyma *and* identify and triage any discrete nodules. Neither task should crowd out the other.

Diffuse thyroid disease is fundamentally a pattern-recognition exercise anchored in physiology. The same physical principles that produce normal thyroid echogenicity explain why autoimmune thyroiditis becomes coarsely hypoechoic, why Graves' disease produces a striking Doppler "inferno," and why subacute thyroiditis generates patchy, avascular geographic lesions. Learn the mechanisms and you can reason through unfamiliar cases rather than simply matching appearances to a memorised list.

One organisational point before we begin: the UK U1–U5 scoring system applies to discrete nodules, not to diffuse parenchymal change. However, nodules and diffuse disease frequently coexist — and a diffusely abnormal gland does not confer protection against malignancy. You must characterise the background *and* rigorously apply U-scoring to any focal lesion. Both tasks matter.

## Sonographic Anatomy and Why Normal Looks Normal

### Architecture and Echogenicity

The thyroid is composed of colloid-filled follicles packed within a fine capillary network and stroma. This dense, uniform microarchitecture backscatters ultrasound efficiently, making the normal gland:

- More echogenic than the adjacent strap muscles — this is your key internal reference point
- More homogeneous and finer in texture than the parotid or submandibular glands
- Smoothly marginated with a thin echogenic capsule

The isthmus lies anterior to the trachea and is typically 2–4 mm thick. A pyramidal lobe extends cranially in a significant minority of patients and is particularly prominent in Graves' disease and in children; recognise it as a normal variant rather than a mass.

### Vascular Supply and Doppler Baseline

The gland receives a rich bilateral supply from the superior and inferior thyroid arteries, forming a subcapsular and intraparenchymal plexus. On colour Doppler at appropriate settings, the normal gland shows:

- Sparse to moderate punctate intraparenchymal flow
- Peripheral subcapsular vessels
- Peak systolic velocities (PSV) in the inferior thyroid arteries typically below 30–40 cm/s, though this is both operator- and device-dependent

Understanding this baseline is essential. Processes that expand the interstitium, replace follicular colloid, or alter vascular tone will predictably change both echotexture and Doppler appearance. Reason from the physiology rather than memorising isolated appearances.

## Technique That Sets You Up to Succeed

### Positioning and Setup

Place the patient supine with a small pad beneath the shoulders to extend the neck gently. Avoid excessive extension in elderly or kyphotic patients, as this causes discomfort and instability. A swallow will mobilise the larynx and trachea if a lesion is difficult to separate from adjacent structures. If compressive symptoms are the clinical question, assess the trachea during quiet respiration and a gentle Valsalva.

### Probe Choice and Machine Settings

A high-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz) is appropriate for most examinations. For large or deep glands, drop to a 9–12 MHz setting. A small-footprint "hockey-stick" probe is useful for the isthmus and for angling around the sternal notch. Compound imaging and tissue harmonic imaging reduce speckle but can obscure microcalcifications — use them judiciously and turn them off when assessing echogenic foci.

Optimise:
- **Near-field TGC:** Elevate to avoid artefactual hypoechogenicity immediately beneath the skin — a gel pad helps if the gland is very superficial
- **Focal zones:** Position at mid-gland depth
- **Dynamic range:** Moderately low, to preserve contrast between parenchyma and pathological change

For colour and power Doppler:
- Low pulse-repetition frequency (PRF), around 0.7–1.2 kHz, and low wall filter to capture slow intraparenchymal flow
- Colour gain set just below the noise threshold
- Power Doppler and microvascular imaging modes are more sensitive for low-velocity flow but are motion-dependent — stabilise your hand and ask the patient to breathe gently

Overcalling vascularity on improperly calibrated Doppler is one of the most common errors in thyroid scanning. Always confirm striking colour findings with spectral waveform analysis.

### Systematic Sweep and Measurements

Scan each lobe in transverse from superior to inferior pole, then in longitudinal from medial to lateral. In the transverse plane, assess contour, echotexture, the isthmus, tracheal alignment, and the strap muscles. In the longitudinal plane, look posteriorly for parathyroid tissue and retrotracheal extension.

Measure each lobe in three orthogonal axes and calculate volume using the ellipsoid formula:

**Volume (ml) = AP × ML × CC (all in cm) × 0.52**

Report the isthmus thickness at the midline. In iodine-replete populations, approximate upper limits of normal are around 18 ml in women and 25 ml in men, but there is genuine biological and regional variation. Report the number and let the clinician and context determine whether it constitutes pathological enlargement.

Crucially, always survey the central compartment (level VI) and lateral neck (levels II–V) for nodes, even when the primary question is about the thyroid parenchyma. Thyroid disease and nodal pathology frequently coexist.

## How Disease Alters Appearance: The Core Principles

Before cataloguing individual conditions, it is worth fixing the underlying mechanisms in your mind:

| Pathological process | Mechanism | Ultrasound consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Cellular infiltrate + oedema | Reduces average acoustic impedance | Hypoechogenicity; loss of fine homogeneity |
| Lymphoid hyperplasia + fibrosis | Disrupts microarchitecture; attenuates beam | Coarse heterogeneity; echogenic septa; vascular pruning |
| Autoimmune-mediated vasodilation | Markedly increases capillary blood flow | Diffuse parenchymal hyperaemia ("inferno") |
| Destructive follicular injury | Oedema + capillary disruption | Patchy ill-defined hypoechoic areas with reduced local perfusion |
| Dense fibrosis | Strong beam attenuation and scatter | Posterior acoustic shadowing; ill-defined margins |
| Colloid accumulation + nodular hyperplasia | Macrofollicle formation; haemorrhage; fibrosis | Heterogeneous echotexture; comet-tail artefacts; variable calcifications |

Use these mechanisms as your navigational compass when encountering unfamiliar appearances.

## A Pattern-Based Approach to the Diffusely Abnormal Gland

### 1. Diffuse Enlargement with Striking Hypervascularity: Graves' Disease

**Grey-scale appearance:** The gland is diffusely enlarged, usually symmetrically so, with a thickened isthmus. The parenchyma is mildly hypoechoic but relatively homogeneous, particularly early in the disease. Margins are smooth. A prominent pyramidal lobe is common.

**Doppler:** This is the defining feature. Diffuse parenchymal hyperaemia — the "thyroid inferno" — fills both lobes at low PRF settings. The inferior thyroid artery PSV is typically elevated above 40–60 cm/s, often substantially so, with a low resistive index reflecting the markedly reduced peripheral vascular resistance.

**Clinical and biochemical context:** Suppressed TSH; elevated fT4 and/or fT3; positive TSH-receptor antibodies (TRAb). The diagnosis is fundamentally biochemical and clinical; ultrasound is not required to diagnose Graves' disease but is helpful when scintigraphy is contraindicated (for example, in pregnancy) or when the diagnosis is genuinely uncertain.

**Why it looks this way:** TRAb-mediated stimulation of TSH receptors increases capillary density and blood flow; interstitial oedema mildly reduces echogenicity.

**Key differentials:**
- *Early Hashimoto's thyroiditis* can be hypervascular, but the hyperaemia is less uniform and the parenchyma is typically more heterogeneous. TRAb is the decisive laboratory discriminator.
- *Toxic multinodular goitre* shows patchy vascularity concentrated around autonomous nodules with a heterogeneous, nodular background rather than uniform global hyperaemia.

**Pitfall:** Over-gain or excessively low PRF creates false "inferno" appearances. Always confirm with spectral waveform measurement. Elevated PSV in the inferior thyroid artery on spectral Doppler is strong supportive evidence.

### 2. Heterogeneous Hypoechoic Gland with Pseudonodularity: Autoimmune Thyroiditis

This category encompasses Hashimoto's thyroiditis, painless (silent) thyroiditis, and postpartum thyroiditis. All share a common pathological substrate — lymphoplasmacytic infiltration — though they differ in clinical course and serology.

**Grey-scale appearance:** The gland is diffusely hypoechoic relative to the strap muscles. The echotexture is coarse and heterogeneous, with tiny hypoechoic lobules separated by echogenic fibrous septa — described variably as a "micronodular," "giraffe-pattern," or "pseudolobulated" appearance. In chronic or fibrotic disease, linear echogenic strands become prominent, volume decreases (atrophic Hashimoto's), and vascularity diminishes.

**Doppler:** Variable. In active early-phase disease, parenchymal vascularity may be increased. In established fibrotic disease, flow is often reduced or sparse.

**Why it looks this way:** Lymphoid infiltration and oedema lower the average acoustic impedance of the parenchyma; lymphoid follicle formation and fibrous septation create the characteristic pseudolobulated architecture.

**Pseudonodules — a critical concept:** The hypoechoic lobules in thyroiditis are *not* true nodules. They have no capsule, their margins are ill-defined and blend imperceptibly with the background, and they lack internal vascularity. They may wax and wane over weeks to months. Do not assign U-scores to pseudonodules. A practical tip: a true nodule persists in both orthogonal planes as a discrete, defined structure; a pseudonodule often "blends away" as you alter your angle. Cine review in two planes is more reliable than static images.

**Coexisting true nodules and cancer risk:** Hashimoto's thyroiditis is associated with a modestly increased prevalence of papillary thyroid carcinoma and a significant association with thyroid lymphoma. The background thyroiditis does not change how you U-score a discrete lesion — do not upstage or downstage based on the background. Apply the U-score as you would in any other gland.

**Lymph nodes:** Prominent level VI and lateral reactive nodes with preserved echogenic hila are common in active thyroiditis. These are reactive and should not be overcalled as metastatic. However, maintain a low threshold for targeted FNA of any node that looks overtly abnormal — loss of hilum, rounded shape, microcalcification, or cystic change should prompt further assessment.

**When to sample:** Diffuse parenchymal change alone does not warrant FNA. Perform FNA of any discrete nodule meeting U4–U5 threshold criteria per your local protocol. If there is rapid or disproportionate enlargement in the context of known thyroiditis — particularly if the tissue is very hypoechoic and confluent — thyroid lymphoma must be considered. FNA is frequently non-diagnostic for lymphoma; arrange ultrasound-guided **core biopsy** with MDT input to enable immunophenotyping by flow cytometry.

**Pitfall:** Colloid within small follicles produces bright echogenic foci with a posterior **comet-tail or ring-down artefact** — this is a benign finding and should not be reported as microcalcification. True microcalcifications are static punctate echoes *without* posterior artefact that do not change with depth or angulation. When in doubt, vary your focal depth and probe angle: the comet-tail will change; a true calcification will not.

### 3. Patchy, Poorly Vascular, Tender Hypoechoic Areas: Subacute (de Quervain's) Thyroiditis

**Grey-scale appearance:** Ill-defined, "geographic" hypoechoic regions within the gland, frequently asymmetric and multifocal. The areas can be large enough to simulate a nodule or, in severe cases, to involve most of a lobe. The margins are poorly defined and do not correspond to any anatomical boundary within the gland.

**Doppler:** Reduced or absent intralesional flow is the characteristic finding, contrasting with the surrounding relatively normal or peripherally hypervascular parenchyma.

**Clinical context:** This is typically a painful condition — the patient will often volunteer tenderness on palpation of the probe. Elevated ESR and CRP; transient thyrotoxicosis (due to follicular destruction releasing stored hormone) followed by hypothyroidism, then usually recovery. Radioiodine uptake is low, which distinguishes it from Graves' if clinical uncertainty exists.

**Why it looks this way:** Post-viral destructive folliculitis produces focal oedema and capillary injury, reducing local perfusion. The process is heterogeneous and evolves over time — sequential scanning will show migration and resolution of the hypoechoic areas.

**Key differentials:**
- *Suppurative thyroiditis or abscess:* A fluctuant, anechoic or hypoechoic collection with internal debris, peripheral hyperaemia, and possible gas. This is a surgical emergency. Aspirate under ultrasound guidance for Gram stain and culture if suppuration is suspected.
- *Focal autoimmune thyroiditis:* Typically less tender; background diffuse thyroiditis changes present elsewhere in the gland.

**Management implication:** Avoid FNA of painful, avascular geographic lesions unless you suspect suppuration or malignancy. The treatment is medical (NSAIDs or corticosteroids depending on severity), and biopsy is likely to be uninformative and uncomfortable for the patient.

**Special paediatric situation:** Recurrent left-sided suppurative thyroiditis in a child should raise suspicion for a **pyriform sinus fistula** (a congenital tract from the piriform fossa to the thyroid region). Refer to ENT; CT or MRI after the acute episode resolves will define the tract.

### 4. Hard, Infiltrative, Shadowing Gland: Riedel's Thyroiditis

**Grey-scale appearance:** Markedly hypoechoic, ill-defined thyroid tissue with coarse posterior acoustic shadowing that seems disproportionate to the echogenicity — a feature of dense fibrous tissue rather than calcification. Critically, the hypoechoic infiltrate spills beyond the capsule into the perithyroidal soft tissues, obliterating the normal fat planes.

**Doppler:** Intraparenchymal flow is often reduced due to fibrous replacement of the vascular bed.

**Clinical context:** The patient typically presents with a "woody hard" neck, sometimes with dysphagia, hoarseness, or stridor. An association with IgG4-related systemic disease is well recognised.

**Why it looks this way:** Dense infiltrative fibrosis replaces the normal gland and extends into adjacent structures, producing strong beam attenuation and loss of normal margins.

**Action:** This appearance warrants cross-sectional imaging (CT or MRI) to assess the extent of infiltration and any airway compromise. Tissue diagnosis requires **core biopsy** — fine-needle aspiration will yield insufficient material. The critical differential is anaplastic thyroid carcinoma or the sclerosing variant of papillary thyroid carcinoma, both of which may produce a similar clinical and ultrasound picture. This is an urgent MDT decision.
