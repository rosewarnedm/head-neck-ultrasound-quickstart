# Chapter 6: Salivary Gland Ultrasound

---

## 1. Clinical Context and Indications

Salivary gland ultrasound sits at the intersection of several busy clinical pathways: GP neck-lump referrals, ENT outpatient clinics, and the two-week-wait (2WW) suspected head and neck cancer pathway. Understanding *why* the patient has been referred shapes what you are looking for before you pick up the probe.

The commonest presentations you will encounter are:

- A **painless, discrete swelling** of the parotid or submandibular gland — the classic "neck lump" that may be neoplastic, nodal, or a systemic condition
- **Painful, meal-related swelling** — the physiological clue to ductal obstruction; salivary flow stimulated by eating cannot escape past a stone or stricture
- **Recurrent or episodic gland swelling** — consider autoimmune (Sjögren's), IgG4-related disease, or juvenile recurrent parotitis in children
- **Acute sialadenitis or suspected abscess** — ultrasound confirms the diagnosis and guides drainage
- **Oncological indications** — staging of primary salivary tumours, assessing intraparotid nodes in patients with cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) or melanoma, and post-treatment surveillance

Certain features should immediately raise your index of suspicion and either trigger a 2WW referral or prompt MDT discussion if one has not already occurred:

- **Facial nerve palsy or facial paraesthesia** in the context of a parotid mass: this is a red flag until proven otherwise, pointing strongly towards malignancy with perineural involvement
- Rapid growth, fixation to skin or deep tissues, skin ulceration, or trismus
- **Cystic level II nodes** in an older adult — metastatic SCC from a cutaneous primary is the first differential, not a "parotid cyst"
- Intraparotid lymphadenopathy in a patient with known scalp, ear, or temple cutaneous malignancy

Note that the **U1–U5 thyroid scoring system** has no equivalent for salivary pathology. Risk stratification here relies on pattern recognition, clinical context, and tissue diagnosis reported using the **Milan System for Reporting Salivary Gland Cytopathology**.

## 2. Sonographic Anatomy and Why It Looks the Way It Does

Rather than memorising isolated facts, understand why each structure has its characteristic appearance.

### Parotid gland

The parotid is the largest major salivary gland, and its lobules contain abundant **intralobular fat**. This fat creates numerous acoustic interfaces, making the gland relatively **hyperechoic** — often equal to or slightly brighter than adjacent muscle, and generally more echogenic than the submandibular gland. When the gland is diffusely inflamed or infiltrated, this echogenicity is lost and the gland becomes hypoechoic, a straightforward principle that explains appearances across many conditions.

The facial nerve runs between the superficial and deep lobes, but you will **not see it directly** on ultrasound. The practical landmark is the **retromandibular vein** (RMV), which runs in the same fascial plane. Masses that displace the RMV anteriorly or medially should prompt you to suspect deep lobe origin, and that is the cue to escalate to MRI.

**Intraparotid lymph nodes** are normal structures and should not be mistaken for pathology. They are concentrated in the tail and superficial lobe, appear oval with an echogenic fatty hilum and thin hypoechoic cortex, and show hilar vascularity on Doppler.

### Submandibular gland

Denser acinar tissue with less fat produces a **finer, more homogeneous echotexture** and slightly lower echogenicity than the parotid — a direct consequence of tissue composition. **Wharton's duct** emerges at the hilum and travels anteriorly along the floor of the mouth to the sublingual caruncle; it is normally a thin compressible tube and should not be conspicuous unless obstructed.

### Sublingual glands

Small almond-shaped clusters sitting in the floor of the mouth above the mylohyoid. Often difficult to delineate from surrounding tissue unless focally diseased. Relevant mainly when assessing for ranula or ductal pathology.

### Ducts: Stensen's and Wharton's

Both ducts are thin and compressible when normal. **Stensen's duct** runs superficially over masseter before piercing buccinator; asking the patient to gently inflate their cheek displaces the buccinator and helps visualise the distal duct. **Wharton's duct** takes a longer, uphill course — which partly explains why submandibular stones are so much more common than parotid stones (combined with the more viscous, alkaline, mucin-rich saliva).

### Adjacent spaces relevant to interpretation

- The **parapharyngeal space (PPS)** lies deep to the deep lobe; a deep lobe parotid tumour will displace the PPS fat medially, seen on cross-sectional imaging but inferred on ultrasound from RMV displacement
- **Level I nodes** (Ia submental, Ib submandibular triangle) are intimately related to the submandibular gland; distinguishing a submandibular level Ib node from an intraglandular lesion is a core skill
- **Level II nodes** are adjacent to the parotid tail, and differentiating a tail-of-parotid mass from a level II node is one of the most practically important judgements you will make

### Doppler physiology

Normal salivary tissue has low to moderate background vascularity. Inflammation increases colour Doppler fill and reduces arterial resistance indices. Most benign tumours show orderly intralesional vessels. Disordered, peripheral, or penetrating vascularity may suggest malignancy, but overlap is substantial and Doppler alone cannot replace tissue diagnosis.

## 3. Equipment, Patient Positioning, and Technique

### Probe selection

A **high-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz)** is the standard choice for the superficial and tail portions of the parotid and the submandibular gland. For the preauricular region, parotid tail, paediatric patients, and intraoral assessment of Wharton's duct, a **hockey-stick probe (15–18+ MHz)** offers a small footprint and excellent near-field resolution. If the patient has a thick neck or you suspect deep lobe extension, dropping to a **6–9 MHz linear or small curvilinear probe** improves depth penetration.

### Optimising your settings

- Set **focal zones** at the depth of your region of interest
- Use **compound imaging and tissue harmonics** to improve margin definition — this makes a real difference when characterising lesion edges
- Turn off aggressive **noise reduction** when looking for microcalcifications
- For **colour Doppler**: use a low pulse repetition frequency (PRF, around 0.7–1.0 kHz), small colour box, adequate wall filter, and high colour gain to detect low-flow hyperaemia
- For **twinkling artefact** to detect stones: increase colour gain, use a moderate-to-higher PRF, and reduce the wall filter — but treat twinkling as a flag to look harder on B-mode, not as proof of calcification in itself, as rough non-calcified interfaces can also twinkle

### Patient positioning

Supine with slight neck extension; a rolled towel between the shoulders helps. Rotate the head away from the side being examined for submandibular scanning. For Stensen's duct assessment, ask the patient to gently "puff out" their cheek. A **sialogogue** (a drop of lemon juice) can provoke duct dilatation and stone impaction if you suspect obstruction that is not demonstrated at rest.

### Systematic sweep

**Parotid:** Begin preauricularly and sweep transversely and longitudinally down to the angle of the mandible, covering the tail and the superficial lobe. Identify the RMV as your internal landmark. Follow Stensen's duct over masseter if indicated. Always complete a targeted nodal survey of at least levels I–III, and extend to levels IV–VI for oncological referrals.

**Submandibular:** Sweep from the posterior hilum to the anterior gland, assessing the gland's relationship to the posterior belly of digastric and mylohyoid. Track Wharton's duct along the floor of the mouth as far anteriorly as practical; for distal duct stones at or near the papilla, an intraoral hockey-stick approach (with a sterile probe cover) is often necessary.

**Sublingual/floor of mouth:** A submental or paramedian approach works for most ranulas; a plunging ranula extending into the submandibular space may require scanning from below to demonstrate the tail sign. Consider intraoral assessment for ductal stones at the papilla.

### Documentation

Measure any focal lesion in three orthogonal planes. Record margins, echogenicity, internal architecture (solid, cystic, mixed), calcification, posterior acoustic features, relationship to vessels and fascia, and vascular pattern on Doppler. Document the ductal calibre. Never omit the cervical nodal survey.

## 4. Normal Appearances

Knowing the range of normal prevents over-calling pathology.

- **Parotid:** Homogeneous, finely lobulated, mildly hyperechoic to muscle. Intraparotid nodes are oval with a hyperechoic hilum, thin cortex, and hilar vascularity — normal findings, particularly in the tail. Stensen's duct is usually imperceptible unless specifically sought.
- **Submandibular:** Slightly less echogenic than parotid, homogeneous. Wharton's duct appears as a thin compressible line; the hilum contains small vessels and fatty echoes.
- **Sublingual:** Small isoechoic lobules, often only apparent when diseased.
- **Doppler:** Sparse intraglandular colour signal; intraparotid nodes show characteristic hilar flow. Vigorous colour fill in a clinically normal gland should prompt clinical correlation.

## 5. Reasoned Approach to a Salivary Presentation

Rather than approaching every referral with an identical protocol, think in **physiological and anatomical patterns** before you scan. This shapes your search image and prevents cognitive anchoring.

**Painless, slow-growing, well-defined parotid mass in a middle-aged adult:** Most likely a benign neoplasm — pleomorphic adenoma in either sex; Warthin's tumour in an older male smoker. An intraparotid node is also in the differential. Evaluate margins, internal architecture, and nodal landmarks carefully.

**Painful swelling worse at mealtimes:** This is obstructive sialadenitis until proven otherwise. Pursue ductal dilatation, a causative calculus, or a stricture methodically before concluding the duct is normal. Submandibular gland and Wharton's duct should be your primary focus, as stones are commonest there.

**Diffuse gland enlargement without a focal mass:** Broaden your differential. Acute sialadenitis produces diffuse hypoechogenicity and hyperaemia; Sjögren's syndrome creates a characteristic heterogeneous, "moth-eaten" or microcystic pattern across both parotid and submandibular glands; IgG4-related disease tends to cause bilateral, relatively homogeneous submandibular enlargement; sialadenosis produces diffuse but echogenically preserved enlargement with normal Doppler.

**Rapidly enlarging or painful mass with systemic upset:** Think infection/abscess, necrotic node, or high-grade malignancy. Do not delay cross-sectional imaging or FNA when clinically concerned.

**Older adult with a tail-of-parotid mass, smoking history, or multifocal/bilateral lesions:** Warthin's tumour is common, but **intraparotid metastatic nodes** from scalp or ear cutaneous primaries must not be missed. Always enquire about skin malignancy history and scrutinise the skin if accessible.

## 6. Obstructive Sialadenitis and Sialolithiasis

### Why it looks that way

Obstruction raises intraductal pressure, causing upstream ductal ectasia and acinar oedema. The gland becomes hypoechoic and enlarged because oedematous acinar tissue has lost its normal fat-rich interfaces. Increased vascularity reflects the inflammatory response. Chronically obstructed glands develop fibrosis, which produces coarse echogenic strands and intraglandular ductal ectasia.

**Submandibular calculi are more common** because Wharton's duct is long, takes an uphill course, and the saliva is more viscous, alkaline, and calcium-rich. Parotid calculi are smaller and less frequent; strictures are relatively more important in the parotid system.

### Ultrasound findings

- **Dilated duct:** An anechoic or echo-poor tubular structure; calibre should reduce with massage or sialogogue provocation if the obstruction is intermittent
- **Calculus:** Echogenic focus with posterior acoustic shadowing; small stones may not shadow convincingly but will show colour **twinkling** on Doppler — treat this as a reason to look more carefully on B-mode, not as definitive proof
- **Gland:** Enlarged and hypoechoic acutely; heterogeneous with echogenic fibrotic strands and intraglandular ectasia in chronic disease
- **Abscess:** A hypoechoic or anechoic collection with internal debris, peripheral hyperaemia, and occasionally echogenic foci with dirty shadowing if gas is present

### Technique tips

Track the entire duct length systematically — stones are often multiple and distal calculi at the papilla require intraoral assessment with a high-frequency hockey-stick probe. Gently "milk" the duct from distal to proximal whilst scanning; this may move a mobile stone into view or cause expression of debris. If twinkling is your primary clue, switch to B-mode and adjust depth, angle, and gain to demonstrate shadowing before committing to a stone diagnosis.

### Key differentials

- **Phleboliths** within a venous malformation: echogenic with shadowing, but situated within a soft-tissue vascular lesion showing slow venous flow on Doppler — the context is usually obvious
- **Air bubbles** post-procedure: transient echogenic foci without convincing shadowing, and mobile with position or gentle compression

### When to escalate

Refractory obstruction or suspected stricture without an obvious stone warrants **sialendoscopy** (diagnostic and therapeutic) or **MR sialography** for non-invasive ductal mapping. Complicated infection, abscess formation, or clinical failure to improve should prompt CT for abscess extent and stone mapping, with ENT involvement for drainage.

## 7. Infective and Inflammatory Conditions

### Acute bacterial sialadenitis

The affected gland is enlarged, diffusely hypoechoic, and intensely hyperaemic on colour Doppler, reflecting the acute inflammatory physiology. Ductal dilatation suggests an obstructive component — always look for a causative stone or stricture, as treating the infection without addressing obstruction leads to recurrence. Microabscesses appear as small, ill-defined hypoechoic foci; a frank abscess shows a rim of hyperaemia and may contain debris or gas.

### Viral sialadenitis

Mumps causes bilateral parotid enlargement with diffuse hypoechogenicity and hyperaemia, often with reactive cervical lymphadenopathy. Imaging is rarely required clinically but the appearances are typical when performed.

### Chronic sialadenitis

Post-obstructive or recurrent sialadenitis leaves the gland heterogeneous with coarse echogenic fibrotic strands, intraglandular ductal ectasia, and sometimes punctate calcifications representing microliths. Vascularity is variable.

### Juvenile recurrent parotitis

Children with episodic parotid swelling. During episodes the gland shows multiple small hypoechoic "microcysts" and increased vascularity. Between episodes the gland may appear near-normal or retain a speckled heterogeneous pattern. Avoid invasive biopsy; management is conservative, and ultrasound is the monitoring tool of choice.

### HIV-associated benign lymphoepithelial lesions

Parotid enlargement with multiple cystic lesions and solid lymphoid tissue; associated intraparotid and cervical lymphadenopathy. The clinical context (HIV status, bilateral involvement) distinguishes this from multicystic metastatic disease, but correlation is essential.
