# Chapter 2: Reporting and the Multidisciplinary Context

## Why Reporting Matters in Head and Neck Ultrasound

Head and neck ultrasound sits at the front door of the cancer pathway. A GP refers a 55-year-old smoker with a three-week neck lump; an ENT consultant wants nodal staging before listing for panendoscopy; an endocrinologist asks whether a palpable nodule needs biopsy. In each case, what you write directly determines the urgency of the next step, whether that step is FNA, MRI, or reassurance, and how the multidisciplinary team plans treatment.

A good report does three things. First, it answers the clinical question — or, where necessary, reframes it. Second, it communicates risk and uncertainty in language the MDT can act on. Third, it makes recommendations that fit local pathways. Reciting measurements without interpretation serves none of these purposes. The discipline of head and neck reporting is learning to link sonographic appearances to probable biology, express your level of confidence, and translate that into a clear clinical recommendation.

## Core Principles for Head and Neck Ultrasound Reporting

### 1. Anchor every report in the clinical context

Before you put probe to skin, know what you have been asked to exclude. The relevant details differ by setting:

- **GP neck-lump referral**: age, duration, prior infections, smoking history, HPV risk (especially relevant in the 40–60 age group with oropharyngeal symptoms), dental problems. The question is usually "is this benign or does it need a two-week-wait referral?"
- **Head and neck oncology staging or surveillance**: known primary site and stage, side, prior treatment. Your task is nodal mapping by level, documentation of morphological features suggesting metastasis or extranodal extension, and identification of the safest biopsy target.
- **Thyroid or endocrine clinic**: palpable versus incidentally found nodule, compressive symptoms, thyroid function, personal or family history of thyroid cancer, prior neck irradiation. Your task is to assign a U score and guide the biopsy decision.

State in the body of the report what you were asked to address. It anchors your impression, makes clear that you understood the referral, and protects you if findings later turn out to be discordant with the clinical picture.

### 2. Describe what you see; interpret what it means

A report that says "there is a 14 mm node in level II with loss of the fatty hilum and peripheral vascularity" is better than one that says "there is a 14 mm node in level II." But neither is complete without the interpretive step: these features reflect disruption of normal nodal architecture by tumour infiltration — the hilar fat is effaced by neoplastic cells, neovascularity supplies the tumour from the periphery rather than through the hilum. Explaining the biological logic — briefly — helps the clinician reading your report generalise to borderline cases and helps you remember what you are actually looking for.

The same principle applies in thyroid reporting. Microcalcifications correspond to psammoma bodies within papillary carcinoma. A taller-than-wide orientation implies a nodule that has grown across tissue planes rather than along them, the pattern of an infiltrative lesion. Spongiform echotexture (aggregated microcystic spaces) reflects benign colloid-filled follicles. These are not arbitrary checklists; each feature tells you something about what the lesion is made of.

### 3. Use a structured, consistent format

Consistency matters for two reasons: it ensures you do not miss findings, and it makes your reports readable and comparable over time. Use the same headings every report. Annotate images with side and level. Measure thyroid nodules in three orthogonal planes. Measure nodes by short axis (the short axis better reflects true nodal size and is less affected by tangential imaging). Assign a U score for every thyroid nodule you describe. Map cervical nodes by level and laterality so surgeons and oncologists can plan neck dissection or radiotherapy fields accurately.

### 4. Communicate risk, uncertainty, and next steps explicitly

Avoid leaving the referrer to infer what should happen next. "Indeterminate" is an acceptable conclusion provided it is followed by a plan: *"Appearance is indeterminate — US-guided FNA performed today; if non-diagnostic, recommend core biopsy and MDT discussion."* When findings mandate urgent escalation, say so explicitly: *"Meets criteria for two-week-wait referral; ENT team informed."* When discharging a patient with a benign-appearing lump, safety-net: *"No suspicious features identified; if the lump fails to resolve within four to six weeks, or enlarges, re-refer via two-week-wait pathway."*

### 5. Acknowledge limitations honestly

Ultrasound cannot reliably assess the retropharyngeal space, the parapharyngeal space, the deep lobe of the parotid, or nodes obscured by the mandible or clavicle. When these areas are clinically relevant, say so, and recommend contrast-enhanced CT or MRI as appropriate. Acknowledging a limitation is a clinical judgement, not a failure — it signals to the referring team that further imaging is genuinely needed rather than reflexively requested.

## A Practical Structured Report

A consistent structure need not be lengthy. The following headings cover the essentials:

- **Clinical details**: referral source, indication, symptoms, risk factors, relevant examination findings.
- **Technique**: high-frequency linear transducer (specify if you dropped to a lower frequency), Doppler used, patient position, any limitations (dressings, restricted neck movement, body habitus).
- **Comparison**: prior ultrasound, CT, MRI, or PET-CT where available.
- **Findings**, organised by region:
  - *Lymph nodes*: level and side; short-axis measurement; shape; cortex thickness and symmetry; hilum; echotexture; calcification; necrosis or cystic change; margins; matting; vascular pattern.
  - *Thyroid*: background echotexture; each nodule with three-plane measurements, U score, and key features; extrathyroidal extension; suspicious cervical nodes.
  - *Salivary glands*: gland echotexture; focal lesions (size, margins, solid versus cystic, vascularity); ductal dilatation; calculi (size and distance to papilla).
  - *Other lesions*: cystic versus solid; relation to midline structures; mobility with swallowing or tongue protrusion; vascularity.
- **Impression**: a synthesised diagnosis or differential with stated level of suspicion.
- **Recommendations**: FNA or core biopsy, cross-sectional imaging, two-week-wait referral, interval follow-up, MDT discussion.

Keep language concise. Avoid vague descriptors — "prominent" without qualification is meaningless. If you mean a node is borderline in size but morphologically benign, say that.

## The Multidisciplinary Context in UK Practice

### Two-week-wait and urgent pathways

Several presentations warrant immediate escalation rather than routine follow-up, and your report should make this explicit:

- An **adult lateral neck cyst** should be treated as a cystic nodal metastasis until proven otherwise. In a smoker, drinker, or anyone with oropharyngeal symptoms, this is a two-week-wait referral for suspected head and neck cancer. Where service design permits, same-day ultrasound-guided aspiration and ENT review shortens the pathway.
- A **non-resolving neck lump of more than three weeks' duration**, persistent hoarseness, unilateral otalgia with odynophagia, progressive dysphagia, or unexplained unilateral serous otitis media in an adult: if ultrasound findings are suspicious or indeterminate, document and trigger the two-week-wait pathway.
- **Thyroid nodules** are managed according to British Thyroid Association guidance using the U scoring system. A suspicious cervical node adjacent to a thyroid nodule warrants expedited MDT discussion regardless of the nodule's own size or U score.

When you trigger a two-week-wait referral, document it in the report. If you have spoken to the ENT or head and neck team directly, record who you spoke to and when.

### The head and neck MDT

The head and neck MDT typically includes ENT surgeons, oral and maxillofacial surgeons, clinical and medical oncologists, a head and neck clinical nurse specialist, radiologists, pathologists, speech and language therapists, and dietitians. Your report feeds this discussion. A nodal map with levels, sizes, and the safest biopsy target is more useful to a surgeon planning selective neck dissection than a prose description of multiple enlarged nodes. Annotated images and, where available, a node-level diagram improve communication further.

The thyroid MDT — endocrine surgeons, endocrinologists, radiologists, pathologists — works best when your report includes the U score, the three-dimensional measurements, the key features driving that score, and a clear statement of how cytology results should be managed. If you performed FNA on the day, say so; if results are awaited, indicate when and where they will be reviewed.

Document critical findings — a highly suspicious mass in a previously unknown patient, an acutely infected gland with abscess — and the person you communicated them to, with the time and date.

## Nodal Reporting: Levels, Lexicon, and Reasoning

### Cervical nodal level nomenclature

The American Head and Neck Society level system is used in UK MDT practice and should be used consistently for all nodal reporting:

- **Level I**: submental (IA) and submandibular (IB) triangles, above the hyoid and anterior to the posterior border of the submandibular gland.
- **Level II**: upper internal jugular chain from the skull base to the level of the hyoid. IIA lies anterior to the plane of the spinal accessory nerve (SAN); IIB lies posterior. On ultrasound, IIB is inferred by posterior location near the deep margin of the sternocleidomastoid — a subtle distinction in practice, but worth noting when nodes are present in this position.
- **Level III**: mid internal jugular chain, hyoid to the inferior margin of the cricoid.
- **Level IV**: lower internal jugular chain, cricoid to the clavicle.
- **Level V**: posterior triangle. VA is above the level of the cricoid (along the SAN); VB is below (along the transverse cervical vessels).
- **Level VI**: central compartment — prelaryngeal (Delphian node), pretracheal, and paratracheal nodes.

Annotate the level on each representative image you save and include it in the written description. A node described as "right level IIa, 15 mm short axis, rounded, absent hilum" gives an ENT surgeon immediate anatomical and oncological context.

### What to document for every abnormal node

For each suspicious or abnormal node, document systematically:

- **Short-axis measurement** in millimetres; note the long axis if needed for ratio calculations.
- **Shape**: oval (long-to-short axis ratio greater than approximately 2) or rounded.
- **Cortex**: thickness in millimetres; symmetric or asymmetric; focal eccentric bulge.
- **Hilum**: present and echogenic; displaced; effaced; absent.
- **Echotexture**: homogeneous hypoechoic versus heterogeneous; central necrosis or cystic change.
- **Calcification**: microcalcifications (punctate, no posterior shadowing or subtle shadowing) versus coarse macrocalcification.
- **Margins**: smooth and well defined versus indistinct or spiculated; perinodal fat change.
- **Matting**: coalescence of adjacent nodes.
- **Vascularity**: hilar (normal pattern), peripheral, mixed, or chaotic on colour or power Doppler.

The logic underpinning these features is worth making explicit: early metastatic involvement disrupts nodal architecture — the echogenic hilar fat is replaced, the cortex thickens focally or becomes uniformly hypoechoic, and tumour neovascularity creates peripheral rather than hilar flow. Structural breakdown produces necrosis or cystic change. Extranodal extension disrupts the capsule and infiltrates the perinodal fat. A node that retains a thin symmetric cortex (ideally ≤3 mm), a central echogenic hilum, and hilar-pattern vascularity is behaving like a reactive node, regardless of its absolute size.

This does not mean size is irrelevant — a 25 mm node is statistically less likely to be benign than a 10 mm node even with preserved architecture — but morphology is more important than size when they conflict.

### Common patterns and differentials

**HPV-positive oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma** typically presents with cystic or necrotic nodes at levels II and III, sometimes with a thin rim and internal debris that can look strikingly like a benign branchial cleft cyst. This is a major pitfall — any lateral cystic neck mass in an adult should be presumed malignant until the histology says otherwise. When you aspirate a cystic node, aspirate the fluid completely, then sample the solid rim or wall. Send both. Request HPV DNA or p16 immunocytochemistry as per your local protocol; in EBV-related disease (nasopharyngeal carcinoma), request EBV in situ hybridisation instead.

**Papillary thyroid carcinoma** metastasises to the central compartment (level VI) and the lateral jugular chain (levels II–IV). Look for rounded hypoechoic nodes with microcalcifications and cystic change in these territories. When a thyroid nodule has suspicious ipsilateral nodes, the nodal status affects surgical planning regardless of the nodule's size.

**Tuberculous and atypical mycobacterial lymphadenitis** can produce matted necrotic nodes, sometimes with calcification from healed disease, that are easily mistaken for metastatic disease or lymphoma. Clinical context — travel history, immigrant background, HIV status, lack of a primary head and neck mucosal lesion — is essential. Biopsy should include samples for culture (core biopsy or incision often preferred; inform microbiology).

**Lymphoma** tends to produce multiple, homogeneous, hypoechoic, enlarged nodes with preserved or subtly distorted architecture. In low-grade disease nodes may remain oval; in high-grade disease they may be large and matted. FNA may yield lymphoid cells but is often insufficient for subtype classification. Core biopsy is preferred after MDT discussion, and surgical excision may ultimately be required for architecture-dependent diagnosis.

**Reactive lymphadenopathy** — the most common finding in the GP neck-lump clinic — shows the features described above: oval nodes, thin symmetric cortex, echogenic hilum, hilar vascularity, often bilateral and at levels II–III. Beware: early metastatic nodes can still be oval and may show only subtle focal cortical bulging and mildly increased peripheral flow. If any feature is discordant with a purely reactive picture, document it and lower your threshold for FNA.

### Reporting extranodal extension

Extranodal extension (ENE) upstages the nodal category in the AJCC/UICC system and significantly affects prognosis and treatment planning — surgeons may extend a dissection, and oncologists may intensify postoperative radiotherapy. Ultrasound signs of ENE include an irregular or indistinct nodal contour, apparent capsular breach, hyperechoic perinodal fat stranding, and an obtuse angle of contact between the node and adjacent muscle or skin (normally a sharp angle is preserved). Report your degree of suspicion — "appearances are suspicious for extranodal extension" rather than a categorical statement — because ultrasound has moderate sensitivity and specificity for ENE, and cross-sectional imaging or pathological confirmation is often needed.

### Limitations and escalation

Ultrasound cannot assess retropharyngeal nodes, the parapharyngeal space, or the deep lobe of the parotid. Mandibular and clavicular acoustic shadowing limits visualisation of levels IIB and lower IV/V. When clinical suspicion is high and ultrasound is inadequate, recommend contrast-enhanced CT or MRI explicitly.

## Thyroid Reporting: The U System and the Biopsy Pathway

### Assigning the U score

The British Thyroid Association U scoring system classifies thyroid nodules from U1 to U5 based on ultrasound features. It is the shared language between the radiologist, endocrinologist, and surgeon, and its correct application is one of the most important skills in thyroid ultrasound.

- **U1 — Normal**: no focal lesions; homogeneous echotexture.
