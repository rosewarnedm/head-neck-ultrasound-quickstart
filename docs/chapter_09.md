## Chapter 9: Head and Neck Cancer — Nodal Staging and Surveillance

---

## Why this chapter matters

The two-week-wait neck lump referral is bread and butter for UK head and neck radiology. Most patients either have reactive lymphadenopathy or — in the right clinical context — metastatic squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). Your ultrasound assessment on the day will determine whether someone is reassured and discharged, treated for infection, sampled in the same session, or escalated urgently to cross-sectional imaging and the MDT. Getting this right requires more than pattern recognition; it requires understanding *why* nodes look the way they do, how nodal anatomy relates to likely primary sites, and how treatment changes the picture you are looking at.

## Sonographic anatomy and lymphatic logic

### Lymph node architecture: why nodes look the way they do

Everything you see on ultrasound follows from two structural compartments.

The **cortex** is lymphocyte-rich with little variation in acoustic impedance between its components, so it appears uniformly hypoechoic. The **hilum** contains fat, blood vessels, and lymphatic sinuses — multiple interfaces that generate echoes — so it appears echogenic. Hilar vessels (feeding artery and draining vein) are readily demonstrated on colour Doppler with appropriate settings.

Pathological processes alter this normal architecture in predictable ways:

- **Reactive hyperplasia** thickens the cortex but preserves the overall architecture: the hilum remains visible and hilar flow is maintained. The node may become tender.
- **Metastatic disease** enters via afferent lymphatics and initially lodges in the subcapsular sinus. Early on you may see only a focal eccentric cortical bulge or asymmetric thickening. As tumour burden increases, the hilum is progressively effaced, the node rounds up, and neovascular peripheral flow replaces orderly hilar branching. Central necrosis appears as irregular hypo- or anechoic areas when the node outgrows its blood supply.
- **Lymphoma** replaces normal architecture with a more homogeneous infiltrate — nodes become markedly hypoechoic, almost "black", with relatively sharp margins. Because the architecture is disrupted uniformly rather than focally, necrosis is unusual at presentation but internal vascularity is often prominent.

Understanding these steps helps you reason through ambiguous cases rather than applying criteria mechanically.

### Cervical levels and drainage patterns

Use the standard level I–VI nomenclature throughout your reports; it communicates anatomical location precisely to surgeons, oncologists, and radiologists reading cross-sectional imaging.

| Level | Anatomical boundaries | Typical primary sites draining here |
|---|---|---|
| IA (submental) | Between anterior bellies of digastric, above hyoid | Anterior floor of mouth, lower lip, anterior tongue tip |
| IB (submandibular) | Submandibular triangle | Oral cavity, lips, anterior nasal cavity, submandibular gland |
| II (upper jugular) | Skull base to inferior margin of hyoid, anterior/posterior to spinal accessory nerve (IIa/IIb) | Oropharynx, posterior oral cavity, parotid, nasal cavity |
| III (mid-jugular) | Hyoid to inferior margin of cricoid | Hypopharynx, larynx |
| IV (lower jugular) | Cricoid to clavicle | Larynx, cervical oesophagus, thyroid, lower hypopharynx |
| V (posterior triangle) | Posterior to SCM, anterior to trapezius (Va/Vb) | Nasopharynx, posterior scalp, skin, thyroid |
| VI (central compartment) | Prelaryngeal, pretracheal, paratracheal | Thyroid, larynx, subglottis |

Retropharyngeal nodes lie deep to the pharyngeal wall and are inaccessible to routine ultrasound — they require MRI or CT, and you should flag this limitation explicitly in reports when the clinical question involves the nasopharynx or posterior oropharynx.

Knowing drainage patterns helps you look in the right place. A cystic level II node in a 55-year-old is not a branchial cleft cyst until proven otherwise; it is a metastatic HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC until excluded. A cluster of microcalcified lateral neck nodes in levels III–IV with a normal-feeling thyroid directs you to scrutinise the gland for an occult papillary carcinoma.

## Equipment, patient set-up, and scanning technique

### Probe selection

Use a high-frequency linear array (10–18 MHz) for the majority of your work. For deep level IV and V nodes in a large or muscular neck, step down to 7–9 MHz to preserve penetration. A small-footprint "hockey-stick" linear probe is invaluable in the submental space, parotid tail, and around the mandible. The supraclavicular fossa and low central compartment occasionally need a curvilinear 5–9 MHz probe.

Start with a vascular or thyroid preset. Set the colour Doppler pulse-repetition frequency (PRF) low — around 3–6 kHz — to detect slow hilar flow; you can increase it transiently near the carotid to avoid aliasing. Power Doppler is more sensitive for slow flow and does not show direction, which makes it useful when you want to demonstrate that flow exists in a small node but is not essential for characterisation.

### Patient positioning

Supine with the neck extended, a thin pillow or rolled towel under the shoulders, and the head turned away from the side you are examining. For the submental space (level IA), ask the patient to protrude the tongue slightly to relax the mylohyoid and deepen the space between the chin and the hyoid.

### A systematic sweep

Develop a reproducible sequence and stick to it. Missing nodes is usually a failure of method, not anatomy. One approach:

1. **Level IA**: transverse and longitudinal sweeps between the anterior bellies of digastric, above the mylohyoid.
2. **Level IB**: around the submandibular gland; identify the facial artery (pulsatile) and facial vein; distinguish intraglandular nodes from glandular pathology.
3. **Level II**: start at the angle of the mandible, identify the internal jugular vein (IJV) and carotid, trace inferiorly; sweep deep to the sternocleidomastoid (SCM); look for cystic nodes hiding under the parotid tail; mentally note the spinal accessory nerve plane separating IIa (anteromedial) from IIb (posterolateral).
4. **Level III**: continue along the IJV to the level of the cricoid; sweep medial to lateral.
5. **Level IV**: from cricoid to clavicle; include the supraclavicular fossa and note the thoracic duct on the left.
6. **Level V**: posterior triangle posterior to SCM; cover Va superiorly and Vb inferiorly.
7. **Level VI**: central compartment; use gentle graded compression to displace air from the tracheoesophageal groove; identify prelaryngeal (Delphian), pretracheal, and paratracheal nodes.

Scan each level in two orthogonal planes. The short-axis diameter is your primary measurement for suspicious nodes — it correlates better with pathological involvement than the long axis. Document the relationship of any abnormal node to named vessels, muscles, and skin; annotate the level on saved images.

## What to assess in every node

### Grey-scale criteria

Assess these features together; no single criterion is diagnostic in isolation.

**Size**: Short-axis diameter is the primary metric. Do not apply a single cut-off uncritically — level II nodes can be large and benign in a young person after tonsillitis, while a 7 mm round node without a hilum at level IV in a smoker is suspicious. Size sets the context; architecture makes the call.

**Shape**: Oval (long-to-short axis ratio >2, or S/L ratio <0.5) favours benignity; round (S/L ratio ≥0.5) raises concern. Rounding also occurs in reactive nodes and is therefore a supporting feature rather than a decisive one.

**Hilum**: A visible echogenic hilum is the single most reassuring feature of a benign node. Its absence is suspicious, though very small benign nodes may genuinely not show a hilum on ultrasound because of resolution limits — interpret accordingly.

**Cortex**: A thin, symmetric cortex (typically under 3 mm) is normal. Eccentric or focal cortical thickening, or a focal bulge distorting the capsule, is the earliest morphological sign of subcapsular metastatic deposit and should prompt careful Doppler assessment.

**Echotexture**:
- Reactive: homogeneous hypoechoic cortex.
- Metastatic SCC: heterogeneous; irregular anechoic or hypoechoic areas indicating necrosis.
- HPV-positive oropharyngeal metastasis: may be predominantly cystic with a thin wall and minimal solid component.
- Lymphoma: strikingly homogeneous, deeply hypoechoic, sharp margins; necrosis is unusual.
- Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) metastasis: echogenic punctate foci (psammomatous microcalcifications); cystic change is common and highly characteristic.

**Calcification**: Microcalcifications in a lateral neck node are highly specific for thyroid carcinoma and should prompt meticulous thyroid evaluation. Coarse calcification appears in chronically treated nodes and granulomatous disease (TB, sarcoid).

**Perinodal change and matting**: Loss of the echogenic fat plane between nodes, echogenic stranding of surrounding soft tissue, and nodes fixed together ("matted") are imaging correlates of extracapsular spread (ECS). Recognise and report these explicitly.

**Skin interface**: Tethering, dermal thickening, and sinus tracts raise concern for cutaneous SCC or melanoma metastasis, or for granulomatous infection (TB with collar-stud abscess).

### Doppler assessment

**Pattern matters more than indices**: Orderly hilar branching vascularity is reassuring; peripheral, chaotic, or mixed vascularity is suspicious. Absent Doppler signal in a hypoechoic area confirms necrosis. Resistive and pulsatility indices show too much overlap between benign and malignant nodes to be diagnostically useful on their own.

**Technique pitfalls to avoid**: Excessive transducer pressure compresses veins and slow-flow hilar vessels — use the lightest touch that maintains contact. Over-gain creates colour noise mimicking vascularity; optimise carefully. Do not conclude that a node is avascular because Doppler is absent in a cystic/necrotic area — sample the solid component instead.

### Elastography

Elastography is an optional adjunct, not a substitute for morphology and clinical context. Metastatic nodes tend to be stiffer (higher strain or shear-wave velocity) than reactive nodes; lymphomatous nodes are intermediate; cystic metastases are soft and may paradoxically appear "benign" elastographically. Use it as a tie-breaker when B-mode and Doppler are equivocal, and acknowledge it in your report accordingly.

## Distinguishing benign from malignant: reasoning in practice

Apply these features as a gestalt, weighed against clinical context:

- Preserved hilum + oval shape + symmetric cortex + hilar flow = reassuringly benign pattern; reserve biopsy for cases where clinical suspicion is high despite this.
- Absent hilum + round shape + eccentric cortex + peripheral vascularity = suspicious; proceed to biopsy.
- Cystic lateral neck mass in any adult = metastatic disease until proven otherwise; branchial cleft cyst is a diagnosis of exclusion and requires tissue confirmation.
- Microcalcifications in a lateral node + apparently normal thyroid on palpation = look harder at the thyroid on ultrasound for an occult PTC; add thyroglobulin washout to any aspirate.
- Uniformly hypoechoic, non-necrotic, multiple nodes across levels with preserved oval shape = think lymphoma; do not dismiss as "reactive but big".

## Patterns by tumour type

### Squamous cell carcinoma

**HPV-negative** (oral cavity, larynx, hypopharynx): nodes predominantly at levels II–IV; solid or necrotic; heterogeneous; extracapsular spread is common in bulky disease and has major staging implications.

**HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC**: the most important pattern to recognise in contemporary UK practice. Nodes are often at level II, frequently predominantly cystic with a thin wall and minimal mural nodularity. The danger is misclassifying them as benign cysts. Features that should raise your suspicion: bilateral or multiple nodes along the jugular chain, internal septation, an eccentric mural nodule, and additional morphologically abnormal nodes at other levels. The primary tumour in the ipsilateral tonsil or tongue base may be small and impalpable — your report should prompt targeted endoscopic assessment.

### Papillary thyroid carcinoma

Metastases typically involve level VI first, then levels III–IV, with skip metastases to the lateral neck (levels II–V). The triad of microcalcifications, cystic change with echogenic debris, and peripheral vascularity is highly characteristic. If you aspirate, submit cyst fluid and needle washings for thyroglobulin measurement (and calcitonin if medullary carcinoma is possible). This markedly increases sensitivity over cytology alone.

### Lymphoma

Enlarged, homogeneous, markedly hypoechoic nodes — the so-called "pseudokidney" appearance when the internal architecture is just visible — across multiple levels, often bilateral. Internal vascularity is prominent; necrosis is rare at presentation. **FNA is insufficient for definitive lymphoma subclassification**; request core biopsy and co-ordinate with haematopathology for flow cytometry on fresh material.

### Melanoma and cutaneous SCC

Drainage depends on skin site: posterior scalp to level V, face and anterior scalp to parotid and level I–II, lower lip to level I. Early metastases can present as a subtle focal cortical bulge with peripheral vascularity in an otherwise well-preserved node — this is easily missed without a high index of suspicion and careful systematic scanning in patients with known cutaneous primaries.

### Nasopharyngeal carcinoma

Typically involves retropharyngeal nodes first (invisible to ultrasound), then levels II–V bilaterally. Ultrasound has a limited role in primary staging; MRI is mandatory. Correlate your lateral neck findings with MRI when nasopharyngeal carcinoma is the suspected or known primary.

### Granulomatous infection (TB, atypical mycobacteria)

Matted, necrotic nodes with peripheral hypervascularity; collar-stud abscess formation with sinus tracts to skin; calcification in chronic disease. Do not label these as metastatic SCC without microbiological confirmation, particularly in patients from high-prevalence regions or who are immunocompromised. Submit aspirate for acid-fast bacilli culture and PCR as well as cytology.

## Extracapsular spread: why it matters and what to look for

Clinical or imaging extranodal extension (ENE) is one of the most important variables you can identify on ultrasound because it directly affects staging and treatment planning. Under AJCC 8th edition, ENE at non-oropharyngeal HPV-negative sites can upstage the N category to cN3b. For HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC, ENE does not alter the N category numerically, but it still influences the multidisciplinary treatment discussion.

**Ultrasound signs suggesting ENE**:
- Irregular, spiculated nodal margins; loss of the sharply defined capsule.
- Perinodal fat stranding — echogenic, irregular infiltration of surrounding fatty tissue.
- Nodal contour showing a "bud" of soft tissue extending into adjacent muscle or skin.
- Tethering of overlying skin with dermal thickening.
- Loss of the normal sliding motion of the carotid or IJV during gentle probe pressure — this suggests fixation.
- Encasement or indentation of the carotid artery.

**Pitfalls**: Post-biopsy inflammation can convincingly mimic perinodal stranding; always note if the patient has recently had an excisional biopsy or needle biopsy of the same node. Conversely, bulky reactive nodes can compress adjacent fat planes without true ENE — use the combination of morphological features, not fat-plane compression alone.

When you identify imaging features of ENE, say so clearly in your report, trigger MDT discussion, and recommend cross-sectional imaging if not already performed.
