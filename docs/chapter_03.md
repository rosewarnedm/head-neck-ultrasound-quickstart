# Chapter 3: Thyroid Nodules and Risk Stratification

---

## Why this chapter matters

Thyroid nodules are extraordinarily common — up to half the population harbour one on ultrasound — yet clinically significant malignancy is found in roughly 5–15% of those that come to biopsy. Your job is not to biopsy everything, nor to dismiss nodules reflexively. It is to separate the genuinely suspicious minority from the reassuringly benign majority, select the right nodules for FNA, and communicate findings in a way that supports safe, proportionate surgical and endocrine decision-making.

This chapter teaches you to reason through ultrasound appearances rather than memorise a checklist. It is anchored in UK practice: the British Thyroid Association (BTA) U1–U5 classification, the "Thy" cytology system, image-guided FNA, two-week-wait pathways, and the level I–VI nodal framework used in head and neck oncology.

---

## Clinical context and referral pathways

Understanding who is being referred shapes how you interpret your findings.

**GP "neck lump" referrals** are the bread and butter of thyroid ultrasound. The vast majority will be multinodular goitre, simple cysts, or thyroiditis. A thorough scan with clear U-scoring allows you either to reassure or to flag the small minority who need biopsy.

**ENT and endocrine outpatient lists** tend to be more pre-selected — nodules found clinically or on other imaging, patients with abnormal thyroid function, or those needing pre-operative planning. Here, your mapping of nodule number, location, and relationship to the capsule is operationally important.

**Head and neck oncology** presents a different question: in a patient with biopsy-proven thyroid cancer, you are staging rather than characterising, assessing the central compartment (level VI) and lateral neck (levels II–V) for nodal disease, and sometimes identifying an occult thyroid primary in a patient referred with a suspicious lateral neck node.

**Two-week-wait (2WW) referrals** are triggered by clinical features suggesting malignancy — a rapidly enlarging, hard, fixed thyroid mass; hoarseness or dysphagia from local invasion; or unexplained lateral neck nodes. When you find U5 features or suspicious nodes on a 2WW scan, the report must say so explicitly and recommend the urgent MDT pathway. Importantly, some 2WW lateral neck node referrals will have no obvious nodule in the neck — ultrasound of the thyroid may reveal an occult papillary primary that explains a cystic, microcalcified level III or IV node.

## Sonographic anatomy and its implications

Before scanning, orientate yourself mentally to the anatomy and what can go wrong:

The thyroid occupies the anterior lower neck, the isthmus crossing the 2nd–4th tracheal rings, each lobe lying anterolaterally with the carotid sheath just lateral and the oesophagus posteromedial on the left. The strap muscles (sternohyoid and sternothyroid) lie superficial. Posteriorly, longus colli and the vertebral body form the posterior limit — if a nodule abuts or breaches this plane, extrathyroidal extension is a surgical concern.

The **thyroid capsule** appears as a thin echogenic line. When tumour disrupts it, the sharp interface blurs; this is one of the most important observations you can make. The **recurrent laryngeal nerve** runs in the tracheo-oesophageal groove immediately posteriorly — invasion here causes hoarseness and is an important CT finding, but ultrasound can suggest it if a posterior tumour is tethered to this region.

**Nodal levels relative to the thyroid** matter clinically:
- **Level VI** (central compartment) — bounded by the carotid arteries laterally, hyoid above, and innominate vein below. This is the first echelon nodal basin for papillary carcinoma and is often the level missed on a cursory scan. It requires deliberate assessment.
- **Levels II–IV** (internal jugular chain) and **level V** (posterior triangle) — lateral neck disease usually implies more advanced nodal spread. Cystic or microcalcified nodes here in the right clinical context are characteristic of papillary carcinoma metastasis.

One anatomical differential you must keep in mind is the **parathyroid gland**: an ovoid, homogeneously hypoechoic structure posterior or inferoposterior to the thyroid lobe, with a polar feeding vessel, that moves differently from the thyroid on swallowing. Mistaking an enlarged parathyroid adenoma for an exophytic thyroid nodule is a classic pitfall, with significant clinical consequences.

## Equipment, patient positioning, and scanning technique

**Probe selection:** A high-frequency linear transducer (12–18 MHz) is your standard tool. For the isthmus, upper poles, or children, a small-footprint "hockey-stick" probe gives better access. In large or retrosternal goitres, a lower-frequency curvilinear probe (6–9 MHz) allows greater depth penetration.

**Patient positioning:** Supine with a shoulder roll to extend the neck. A comfortable, moderate extension is better than forced hyperextension, which tenses the strap muscles and can distort appearances. Keep the chin midline and turn the head only minimally when examining the lateral neck.

**System optimisation:**
- Tissue harmonic imaging improves cyst wall definition and reduces artefact — always worth using.
- For colour and power Doppler: narrow the box to the area of interest, reduce pulse repetition frequency (PRF) for slow intrathyroidal flow, and lower the wall filter. Excessive PRF is one of the most common reasons vascularity is under-detected.
- Set the focus at or just below the nodule of interest.

**Systematic scanning approach:** Work through both lobes and the isthmus in a reproducible sequence:

1. **Transverse sweeps** from superior to inferior pole, from the midline laterally to the carotid sheath. On each sweep, identify: trachea (echogenic anterior wall with posterior shadowing), thyroid capsule, strap muscles superficially, carotid artery and internal jugular vein laterally, and the oesophagus posteriorly (typically left of midline; real-time peristalsis or a sip of water confirms its identity — this is how you avoid calling the oesophagus a "left isthmus nodule").
2. **Longitudinal sweeps** along each lobe in medial-to-lateral strips.
3. **Nodal survey:** Deliberately assess level VI (central compartment), then levels II–V bilaterally. This is not optional when you have a U4 or U5 nodule — it is part of the examination.
4. **Save cine loops and orthogonal stills** for every nodule that will be biopsied or put into surveillance.

**Measurements and documentation:** For every significant nodule, record three dimensions (anteroposterior × transverse on the transverse image; length on the longitudinal image), depth from skin surface, and location by lobe, pole, and clockface position. A clear schematic or diagram in the report prevents wrong-site FNA or surgery.

## How ultrasound appearances arise — reasoning from first principles

Understanding why a nodule looks the way it does makes the classification intuitive rather than arbitrary.

**Echogenicity** reflects the ratio of reflecting interfaces to background. Colloid-rich or fibrous tissue scatters sound effectively, producing iso- or hyperechogenicity. A highly cellular tumour with scant colloid (as in papillary carcinoma) contains fewer reflective interfaces and appears hypoechoic. When marked hypoechogenicity — darker than the adjacent strap muscle — is present, the cellularity is particularly high and the risk of malignancy increases substantially.

**Composition** matters because cystic or spongiform architecture is strongly associated with benignity. A purely anechoic cyst with posterior acoustic enhancement and comet-tail artefacts (see below) is effectively never malignant. A spongiform nodule — composed of ≥50% microcysts separated by thin echogenic septa — represents a hyperplastic colloid nodule and carries an extremely low malignancy risk. Solid nodules require more careful scrutiny.

**Comet-tail artefacts vs microcalcifications** is one of the most practically important distinctions in thyroid ultrasound. Comet-tail artefacts are reverberation artefacts from inspissated colloid: they appear as short, tapering, brightly echogenic trails that fan out from a focus and elongate or change character when you adjust focus depth or time-gain compensation. True microcalcifications (psammoma bodies in papillary carcinoma) are fixed, punctate, non-reverberant echogenic foci that do not trail. When you are uncertain, adjust your settings and sweep the probe — the behaviour of the foci usually clarifies matters.

**Margins** reflect the boundary between nodule and normal parenchyma. A smooth margin with a thin, intact hypoechoic halo (representing compressed normal tissue and perinodular vessels) is reassuring. Irregular, microlobulated, or infiltrative margins suggest tumour disruption of normal tissue planes. Spiculated margins are highly suspicious.

**Shape ("taller-than-wide")** is assessed in the transverse plane. A nodule whose anteroposterior dimension exceeds its transverse dimension has grown without the normal constraint of fascial planes — a feature associated with malignancy. This must be measured in a true transverse plane; an obliquely placed probe will give spurious results.

**Calcification patterns** each carry different implications:
- *Punctate microcalcifications* without comet-tail: high suspicion for papillary carcinoma.
- *Peripheral "eggshell" calcification* with an intact rim: relatively benign, often involutional.
- *Interrupted rim calcification* with extrusive soft tissue bulging beyond the calcific shell: this capsular breach is highly suspicious.
- *Coarse macrocalcifications*: non-specific and may be benign, but can shadow and obscure underlying malignant tissue — look carefully at the edges of the acoustic shadow.

**Vascularity:** Doppler is useful but should not drive your classification. Hypervascularity is non-specific — it occurs in malignancy, hyperplasia, and thyroiditis. Hypovascularity does not exclude malignancy. The main clinical uses of Doppler in thyroid nodules are: (1) mapping safe biopsy windows, (2) distinguishing peripheral from internal vascularity as a supporting feature, and (3) identifying the polar feeding vessel of a parathyroid adenoma.

## The BTA U1–U5 ultrasound classification

The BTA system classifies each nodule by its **most suspicious feature**. This is the key principle: you do not average features or take a majority vote. If a nodule has one feature that places it in U4, it is U4 regardless of other benign-looking aspects.

**U1 — Normal**
No focal lesion; normal homogeneous echotexture. This is the appropriate category when the scan is completely normal, not a default for "I can't classify this."

**U2 — Benign**
Features here are those with a very low malignancy risk:
- Purely anechoic cyst with posterior enhancement.
- Cystic or predominantly cystic nodule with comet-tail artefacts from colloid.
- Spongiform architecture (≥50% microcysts).
- Isoechoic or hyperechoic solid nodule with a smooth margin and thin, complete halo.
- Intact peripheral eggshell calcification.
- Mixed cystic-solid with comet-tail foci and no mural nodule.

**U3 — Indeterminate/equivocal**
These nodules have features that are neither clearly benign nor suspicious:
- Solid hypoechoic nodule with smooth margins.
- Mixed echogenicity without high-risk features.
- Lobulated (but not overtly infiltrative) margins.
- Disrupted halo without additional suspicious features.

This category requires a size-dependent biopsy or surveillance decision (see below). U3 is where clinical context and risk factors most influence your recommendation.

**U4 — Suspicious**
- Marked hypoechogenicity (visibly darker than strap muscle).
- Irregular or microlobulated margins.
- Taller-than-wide shape in true transverse section.
- Interrupted rim calcification with extrusive soft tissue.
- Punctate echogenic foci without convincing comet-tail behaviour.

**U5 — Malignant**
- Two or more high-risk features in combination (e.g., marked hypoechogenicity with irregular margins and microcalcifications).
- Frank extrathyroidal extension.
- Suspicious cervical nodes with features of thyroid origin (microcalcifications, cystic change, elevated thyroglobulin on washout) in conjunction with a compatible thyroid nodule.

**A practical note on judgement:** The U system is a framework, not an algorithm. Classification close to category boundaries requires you to weigh the totality of findings. A U3 nodule that is borderline taller-than-wide on one sweep but not on another should probably be re-examined — don't simply pick the most suspicious single image. Conversely, if you genuinely cannot decide between U3 and U4, it is safer to round up and perform FNA rather than defer and miss an early malignancy.

## Selecting nodules for biopsy or surveillance

**The most important principle:** biopsy the most suspicious nodule, not the largest. In a multinodular gland, a 25 mm U2 nodule does not need FNA; a 9 mm U5 nodule does.

These thresholds represent common practice aligned with BTA guidance, but local protocols vary — always follow your Trust's policy:

| Category | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **U2** | No biopsy. Aspirate cysts for symptoms only. Discharge or clinical follow-up unless symptomatic/growing. |
| **U3** | FNA if ≥15 mm. FNA if 10–14 mm with risk factors (prior irradiation, family history of thyroid cancer, age <18 years). Ultrasound surveillance at 12–18 months otherwise. |
| **U4** | FNA if ≥10 mm. Consider FNA <10 mm if worrisome clinical features, proximity to capsule/RLN, suspicious nodes, or paediatric patient. |
| **U5** | FNA regardless of size if technically feasible. Urgent MDT referral / 2WW pathway. Sample suspicious nodes. |
| **Autonomously functioning ("hot") nodule** | Confirm with radionuclide scintigraphy; FNA generally not required given very low malignancy risk. |
| **PET-avid incidental nodule** | FNA if ≥10 mm and solid — malignancy risk is higher than background nodule population. |

**Suppressed TSH:** If TSH is subnormal, arrange scintigraphy before FNA. A concordant hot nodule rarely needs biopsy; a cold nodule in a hyperthyroid patient still warrants consideration.

**Multiple nodules:** Score each individually. When several share the same highest U category, biopsy the one with the most suspicious morphology or the most technically accessible. Document which nodule you are targeting and ensure the histopathologist knows.

**Growth on follow-up:** Clinically meaningful growth is conventionally defined as >20% increase in two dimensions with a minimum absolute increase of 2 mm, or >50% volume increase. However, growth alone does not equal malignancy — re-assess the U category rather than automatically escalating to biopsy. Some genuinely benign nodules grow slowly.

## Practical FNA technique and core biopsy

**Before you start:**
- Review the TSH and clinical background. If the patient is hyperthyroid, scintigraphy should precede FNA for new nodules.
- Check anticoagulation. FNA is low-risk; most units continue aspirin and DOACs with local compression. Follow your departmental policy.

**FNA technique:**
- Use a 25–27G needle, freehand or with a guide, under real-time ultrasound visualisation.
- The capillary (non-aspiration) technique — relying on capillary action by moving the needle back and forth without applying suction — generally yields less blood in highly vascular nodules and is preferred by many operators.
- Take two to three passes from viable solid tissue. Avoid sampling purely cystic components unless the aim is decompression; for cystic nodules with a mural solid component, specifically target that component.
- On-site rapid evaluation of adequacy (ROSE) improves non-diagnostic rates but is not universally available in UK practice. In its absence,
