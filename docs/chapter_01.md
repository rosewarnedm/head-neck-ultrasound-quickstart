# Chapter 1: Scanning Technique and Normal Neck Anatomy

## Introduction and scope

Head and neck ultrasound sits at the heart of everyday radiology practice in the UK. The referrals that drive the workload are familiar: the GP letter describing a "neck lump noticed for three months," the two-week-wait suspected cancer referral from an ENT surgeon, the oncology MDT request for nodal staging, and the outpatient review of a salivary or thyroid problem. All of these depend on the same underlying skill set: knowing how to set up the machine, how to move the probe systematically through the neck, and — crucially — how to recognise normal anatomy so that abnormality stands out.

This chapter establishes those foundations. It covers equipment and machine optimisation, patient positioning, a practical scanning protocol, and the sonographic appearance of the normal neck structures you will encounter every day. Later chapters address the pathological appearances, the U1–U5 thyroid nodule scoring system, and image-guided biopsy technique. The goal here is to explain *why* structures look the way they do, so that you develop transferable pattern recognition rather than relying on memorised lists.

## Principles: why things look as they do

Before discussing anatomy, it is worth grounding the visual appearances in basic physics, because understanding the mechanism helps you solve unfamiliar cases.

**Acoustic impedance mismatches drive reflection.** The greater the difference in acoustic impedance between adjacent tissues, the brighter the reflective interface. Bone and calcified cartilage have very high impedance relative to soft tissue, producing a bright reflector and posterior acoustic shadow. Air — in the pharynx, larynx, and trachea — is an even more dramatic mismatch, causing near-total reflection with reverberation artefact and posterior dropout. Fluid, by contrast, has low attenuation and uniform impedance, so sound passes through easily and you see posterior acoustic enhancement behind cystic structures.

**Tissue microarchitecture determines parenchymal echogenicity.** Fat is echogenic because its multiple small interfaces scatter sound efficiently; muscle is hypoechoic because its long, aligned fibres have few perpendicular interfaces. Glandular organs — thyroid, parotid, submandibular — contain a mixture of acini, ducts, connective tissue, and fat, giving them a characteristic medium-to-high echogenicity that is notably greater than adjacent muscle. This is a useful calibration point: if the thyroid looks no more echogenic than the strap muscles, suspect diffuse parenchymal disease such as thyroiditis.

**Lymph nodes look reniform because of their architecture.** The cortex is composed of lymphoid follicles and is hypoechoic. The hilum contains the fatty medullary sinus and the supplying vessels, producing a central echogenic structure. On Doppler, normal vascularity enters at the hilum and radiates outward. Any node where this architecture is disrupted — replaced cortex, absent hilum, peripheral vascularity — deserves scrutiny.

**Fascial planes compartmentalise disease and organise your mental map.** The investing layer of deep cervical fascia envelops the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) and trapezius. Visceral (pretracheal) fascia surrounds the thyroid, trachea, and oesophagus. Prevertebral fascia envelops the deep paraspinal muscles and brachial plexus roots. On ultrasound these planes appear as thin echogenic lines. Recognising them lets you confidently localise a mass to a specific compartment — a parapharyngeal lesion behaves differently and carries different differentials from a submandibular one, even if they look similar in isolation.

**Motion and compressibility are diagnostic tools.** Veins collapse with gentle probe pressure and vary with respiration. Lymphatic malformations deform with pressure; solid tumours resist it. Asking the patient to swallow shows whether a mass moves with the laryngotracheal complex (suggesting a thyroid or subthyroid origin) or sits independently.

## Equipment and machine settings

### Transducers

Choose your probe based on the depth of the target, not out of habit.

- **High-frequency linear array (12–18 MHz):** your default for the thyroid, salivary glands, and cervical lymph nodes. It provides the spatial resolution necessary to detect microcalcification, thin capsules, and subtle cortical irregularity.
- **Mid-frequency linear (9–12 MHz):** useful for deeper structures — the lower jugular chain (level IV), the posterior triangle (level V) in a muscular patient, and the lower neck in general. Switch down when you are losing penetration.
- **Micro-convex or "hockey-stick" probe (8–12 MHz):** designed for confined spaces — the floor of mouth, the submental triangle, the region close to the mandible or clavicle. It is also used intraorally (with an appropriate sheath) when sublingual pathology requires it.
- **Curvilinear (3–9 MHz):** for retrosternal goitre extension and the thoracic inlet in large patients. You sacrifice resolution but gain penetration.

There is no single "correct" probe choice — experienced operators switch between probes within a single examination. Developing that flexibility comes with practice.

### Presets and key adjustments

Start with the neck or small-parts preset, then optimise for the individual patient.

**Frequency:** use the highest frequency that still provides adequate penetration. Most modern probes allow frequency steering within the transducer's range — push it high for superficial structures and step it down when you lose depth.

**Focus:** position the focal zone at or just below your target and move it as you scan deeper or shallower. A misplaced focus is one of the most common reasons for a blurry image that residents attribute to "the machine."

**Depth:** keep it shallow enough that your target fills a meaningful portion of the screen, but include underlying fascia or bone as a spatial reference. Excessive depth compresses the image and makes subtle findings harder to see.

**Dynamic range:** a moderate setting (roughly 55–70 dB) preserves soft-tissue contrast. Reducing dynamic range can sharpen the conspicuity of calcification but loses subtle texture differences.

**Compound imaging and tissue harmonic imaging** reduce speckle and improve edge definition — helpful for delineating gland margins and nodal capsules. However, they can suppress posterior acoustic enhancement and obscure microcalcification by artificially smoothing the image. Be aware of this when you are specifically looking for these features, and consider toggling them off.

**Near-field:** the isthmus and superficial dermis are prone to near-field artefact. Use generous gel and the lightest possible transducer contact. A gel standoff pad is genuinely useful for very superficial lesions.

### Doppler optimisation

Poor Doppler technique is common and leads to both false negatives (missing flow) and false positives (noise artefact interpreted as vascularity). A few principles help:

- **Colour Doppler** maps flow patterns rapidly. **Power Doppler** is more sensitive to slow flow and is less angle-dependent — useful for assessing a thyroid nodule or a suspicious node. **Spectral Doppler** gives haemodynamic information (waveform shape, resistive index) — use it when characterising carotid disease or when a node's arterial resistance is specifically relevant.
- **PRF (pulse repetition frequency) / velocity scale:** set low (0.5–1.5 kHz) for glands and nodes where flow is slow; higher (3–6 kHz) for carotid arteries. A scale set too high will miss slow parenchymal flow; set too low, it will alias on arteries.
- **Wall filter:** reduce it for low-flow detection in nodes; raise it to suppress motion artefact from vessel walls during carotid assessment.
- **Colour gain:** increase to just below the point where random colour noise appears in vessel-free tissue — then hold there. Excessive gain creates spurious "vascularity"; insufficient gain misses real flow.
- **Transducer pressure:** the single most common Doppler error. Pressing firmly compresses superficial veins and low-flow vessels, abolishing the signal. Hold the probe as lightly as you can while maintaining contact.
- **Angle correction:** for spectral measurements on arteries, correct to 50–60°. Do not attempt angle correction at angles approaching 90° — the error becomes unacceptable.

## Patient preparation and positioning

Explain the examination before starting — patients are often anxious that a neck lump is being scanned, and a brief explanation improves co-operation with dynamic manoeuvres. Ask specifically about prior neck surgery, radiotherapy, or known vascular disease, as these significantly alter the anatomy.

Position the patient supine with the shoulders supported and the neck in mild extension. Rotating the head 30–45° away from the side being examined relaxes the SCM and exposes the lateral neck. Avoid excessive extension, which can be uncomfortable and paradoxically limits access by compressing the posterior triangle.

For submental and floor-of-mouth assessment, ask the patient to open the mouth wide and protrude the tongue. For assessing swallowing-related movement, ask the patient to swallow on command — practice this instruction before starting so it does not break your scanning flow.

Warm the gel; cold gel causes reflex muscle tightening that degrades the image and is simply unpleasant.

## A systematic scanning strategy

Systematic scanning protects against blind spots. The neck is complex, and without a fixed routine you will miss structures or levels. The following protocol is adaptable to clinical context, but the *habit of completeness* is non-negotiable.

### For a GP neck-lump referral

Even when the lump is obvious, examine the whole neck. A "benign-looking" submandibular swelling in a 60-year-old smoker still warrants a full nodal survey and thyroid assessment.

**1. Orient yourself midline first.** Begin with a sagittal midline view: identify the hyoid, thyroid cartilage, cricoid, and tracheal rings, then the thyroid isthmus. This gives you a depth reference and confirms your machine settings are appropriate before you begin the survey.

**2. Thyroid survey.** Sweep each lobe in transverse (superior pole to inferior pole) then sagittal. Assess the isthmus. Note global echogenicity, parenchymal homogeneity, vascularity, and any focal abnormality. U-scoring of nodules is covered in Chapter 3; here the goal is a complete survey.

**3. Salivary glands.** Assess both parotids (transverse and longitudinal from the preauricular region to the mandibular angle, including the tail) and both submandibular glands (longitudinal and transverse, including the posterior free edge of mylohyoid where the deep portion wraps). If duct obstruction is suspected, follow the duct with real-time imaging — Stensen's duct over the masseter for the parotid, Wharton's duct anteromedially along the floor of mouth for the submandibular gland.

**4. Nodal levels I–VI, bilaterally.** Sweep each level systematically in transverse, then confirm suspicious nodes in a second plane. The boundaries (detailed below) translate to specific probe positions on the patient. Do not skip a level because it "looks normal at a glance" — metastatic nodes can be small, isoechoic, and easy to miss.

**5. Targeted assessment of the presenting lump.** Once you have a complete survey, characterise the lump itself: two orthogonal planes with callipers; its relation to fascial layers and adjacent vessels; Doppler; behaviour with swallowing or tongue movement where relevant.

### For oncology staging and two-week-wait referrals

The same survey applies, but the threshold for documentation is lower — record every node that is morphologically abnormal, borderline, or in a level that is at risk given the primary site. Map nodes by level and sublevel (e.g., "right level IIA, short axis 8 mm, absent hilum, peripheral vascularity"). Save cine sweeps rather than single frames to give context.

Note ultrasound's limitations honestly: deep IIB nodes (posterior to the IJV, close to the skull base) and retropharyngeal nodes are not reliably assessed sonographically. Say so in the report and direct the MDT to CT or MRI for these regions.

For post-treatment surveillance, establish a documented baseline early. Fibrosis and scarring create heterogeneous, hypoechoic regions that can mimic — or conceal — residual or recurrent disease. Power Doppler flow within a scar-like area raises suspicion for viable tumour and should prompt discussion about biopsy or cross-sectional imaging.

## Normal neck anatomy on ultrasound

### Skin, subcutaneous tissue, and platysma

The dermis appears as a thin, bright echogenic line. Below it lies lobulated, echogenic subcutaneous fat with thin echogenic septa. The platysma is a thin, hypoechoic sheet running in the subcutaneous tissue anteriorly — it is often not specifically commented upon but provides a depth reference for very superficial lesions.

### Muscles and fascial planes

The **SCM** is your primary landmark throughout the lateral neck. It is a large, fusiform striated muscle running from the mastoid process to the sternum and clavicle. On ultrasound it is hypoechoic with parallel echogenic striations — the classic appearance of skeletal muscle. Its anterior border defines the posterior boundary of levels I–IV; its posterior border defines the anterior boundary of level V.

A critical practical point: **anisotropy** causes muscles (and tendons) to change apparent echogenicity as the beam angle changes. A relaxed, perpendicular scan shows true echogenicity; a tilted probe over a contracted muscle can produce spurious hypoechogenicity that mimics a lesion. Keep the beam perpendicular and ask the patient to relax before concluding that a muscular structure is abnormal.

The **strap muscles** (sternohyoid, sternothyroid, and omohyoid) lie superficial to the thyroid as thin hypoechoic bands. Their echogenicity is a useful calibration: the thyroid should be clearly more echogenic than these muscles.

The **mylohyoid** forms the muscular floor of the mouth — a hypoechoic sling between the mandibular body and the hyoid. The sublingual space lies above it; the submandibular gland wraps around its posterior free edge. This anatomical relationship is clinically important: a simple (plunging) ranula herniates through or around the mylohyoid into the submandibular space, whereas a non-plunging ranula stays above it.

Fascial planes appear as echogenic lines on ultrasound. You may not always see them explicitly, but they organise the anatomy you are looking at. When a mass displaces or infiltrates a fascial plane, that tells you something about its behaviour.

### Salivary glands

**Parotid gland:** The parotid lies superficial and posterior to the mandibular ramus, with its tail descending deep to the SCM. The deep lobe wraps around the mandible into the parapharyngeal space — this portion is largely inaccessible to ultrasound, and you should mention this limitation whenever it is relevant. The gland is homogeneously echogenic (more so than muscle) with lobulated margins. In children it may be relatively hypoechoic because it contains less fat; in elderly patients it becomes more echogenic still as fatty replacement increases.

Stensen's duct courses anteriorly over the masseter muscle and turns medially at the anterior border to enter the buccal mucosa opposite the upper second molar. Normally it is a barely visible, thin anechoic line. When sialectasis is present — from obstruction or chronic sialadenitis — it becomes obviously dilated and readily followed.

**Intraparotid lymph nodes** are a normal finding. Small oval nodes with an echogenic hilum within the parotid parenchyma are common and should not be over-called as tumour. Their normal morphology (oval shape, preserved hilum, hilar vascularity) is the same criterion you apply to nodes elsewhere.

**Submandibular gland:** Located in the submandibular triangle (level IB), with a superficial portion above and a deep portion wrapping around the posterior free edge of mylohyoid. It is slightly less echogenic than the parotid and slightly more echogenic than muscle. Wharton's duct runs anteromedially along the floor of mouth — best seen on a sublingual approach with the patient's tongue raised, particularly if you stimulate salivation.

**Sublingual glands** lie in the sublingual space above the mylohyoid. They are thin, lobulated, and echogenic structures that are not always distinctly delineated transcutaneously. Their importance lies mainly in pathology (ranula, sublingual space masses) rather than in routine normal appearances.
