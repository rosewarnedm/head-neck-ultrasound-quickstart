# Chapter 5: Thyroid Cancer and the Post-Treatment Neck

---

## Why This Chapter Matters

Thyroid ultrasound occupies a central place in everyday UK radiology practice. It sits at the junction of GP neck-lump referrals, ENT outpatient work, and two-week-wait cancer pathways. The vast majority of nodules you encounter will be benign, and part of the skill is providing confident reassurance without unnecessary intervention. The harder part — and the part that genuinely matters to patients — is reliably identifying the minority that harbour malignancy, risk-stratifying them using the U1–U5 system, biopsy guiding when it adds information, and mapping nodal disease accurately when cancer is present or suspected.

After treatment, your role shifts: you become the primary tool for structural surveillance, detecting small-volume recurrence before it is clinically apparent, guiding aspiration for biochemical washout, and helping the MDT decide whether to operate, ablate, or observe. This chapter explains the principles behind what you are seeing, so that the features you encounter on screen make biological sense rather than appearing as an arbitrary checklist.

## Clinical Context and UK Pathways

Most thyroid ultrasound requests in the UK fall into one of three categories.

**GP neck-lump referrals** are the bread and butter. A palpated nodule or incidental finding on another scan prompts an ultrasound, which is risk-stratified using the U1–U5 British Thyroid Association/Royal College of Radiologists (BTA/RCR) system, then either discharged, placed under surveillance, or referred for FNA and MDT review depending on the U score and local thresholds.

**Two-week-wait referrals** are triggered by features suggesting possible malignancy: a rapidly enlarging neck mass, voice change, dysphagia, progressive weight loss, or clinically suspicious lymphadenopathy. Thyroid cancer rarely presents as a true emergency — the exception is anaplastic or poorly differentiated carcinoma, which demands urgent cross-sectional imaging and tissue diagnosis.

**Post-treatment surveillance** follows patients who have undergone hemithyroidectomy or total thyroidectomy, with or without neck dissection and radioiodine (RAI) ablation. Ultrasound forms the structural backbone of surveillance, interpreted alongside serum thyroglobulin (Tg), anti-Tg antibodies, and calcitonin/CEA as appropriate.

MDTs — typically a combined Head and Neck Cancer MDT or a dedicated Thyroid MDT — integrate all of these strands. Your report must give them what they need to act: a clear U score, a nodal map by level, a biopsy plan, and any relevant limitations.

## Sonographic Anatomy and Why It Looks That Way

Understanding the normal appearance starts with understanding the underlying biology.

**The thyroid parenchyma** appears finely echogenic and slightly hyperechoic relative to the adjacent strap muscles. This is because the thyroid is packed with microfollicles separated by thin stromal interfaces, each generating a small acoustic reflection. Disruption of this architecture — whether by inflammation, nodule formation, or infiltrative tumour — alters these reflections predictably.

**The isthmus** is a thin anterior bridge crossing the second and third tracheal rings; in adults it should measure no more than 3 mm in anteroposterior diameter. A thickened isthmus raises suspicion for lymphoma or Hashimoto's thyroiditis.

**The capsule** is a thin echogenic line. Its integrity is important: loss of the capsular reflection over a nodule suggests potential extrathyroidal extension (ETE), which carries staging and surgical implications.

**The trachea** produces strong reverberation artefact anteriorly. Compound imaging and gentle beam angulation help interrogate the tracheo-oesophageal groove, which is the corridor for the recurrent laryngeal nerve and the most common site for central compartment nodal disease.

**The carotid sheath** lies postero-lateral to the thyroid. The common carotid artery (CCA) is non-compressible and pulsatile; the internal jugular vein (IJV) is compressible and phasic. These are your orientation landmarks when scanning the lateral nodal chain.

**Normal lymph nodes** are ovoid, with an echogenic fatty hilum, a thin cortex (short axis typically <5 mm in the lateral neck, <8 mm in Level II), and low-velocity hilar vascularity on Doppler. Understanding *why* malignant nodes look different is more valuable than memorising a list:

- **Microcalcifications** in metastatic papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) are psammoma bodies — laminated calcifications deposited within tumour. They appear as punctate hyperechoic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing and no comet-tail artefact.
- **Cystic change** results from liquefactive necrosis within tumour deposits; in PTC this is particularly characteristic and a cystic lateral neck node in an adult should be considered malignant until proven otherwise.
- **Loss of the fatty hilum** reflects architectural replacement by tumour.
- **Peripheral vascularity** reflects neovascular recruitment from the capsule, the mirror image of the normal central hilar supply.

## Equipment, Positioning, and Systematic Technique

### Equipment

Use a high-frequency linear probe, ideally 12–18 MHz. For the submental region, high Level II, or very superficial nodules, a smaller "hockey-stick" probe improves contact. In a deep or thick neck, stepping down to 9–12 MHz preserves penetration without losing too much detail. A stand-off gel pad helps for very superficial lesions where near-field resolution is limiting. Use compound imaging and tissue harmonic imaging to reduce speckle artefact and improve margin definition.

For Doppler, reduce the pulse repetition frequency (PRF) to 0.5–1.5 kHz and minimise wall filter. Critically, apply minimal transducer pressure — even moderate compression will obliterate low-velocity flow in nodal hilae and tumour neovessels, causing you to miss important information.

### Positioning

Supine, with a pillow or rolled towel under the shoulders to gently extend the neck. For lateral nodal scanning, a slight contralateral head turn opens the jugular chain. Avoid over-extension, which distorts the strap muscles and can make surgical scar tissue difficult to interpret.

### The Systematic Sweep

A disciplined sequence prevents you missing disease.

1. **Thyroid lobes**: transverse sweep from superior to inferior poles, then longitudinal sweeps medial to lateral. Measure each lobe in three planes and note isthmus thickness. Assess background echotexture.

2. **Nodule characterisation**: for each nodule, assign a U score and record location, three-plane size, composition, echogenicity, margin character, shape (taller-than-wide in the AP direction is a significant sign), type of echogenic foci, and any evidence of capsular contact or disruption.

3. **Central compartment (Level VI)**: scan the prelaryngeal (Delphian), pretracheal, and bilateral paratracheal gutters. Use heel-toe probe angulation to look deep to the thyroid and into the groove between the trachea and carotid sheath. These nodes are small, easily overlooked, and clinically important.

4. **Lateral nodal levels II–V**: work in transverse from the skull base down to the clavicle, then confirm in the sagittal plane. Include the posterior triangle and the supraclavicular fossa. In a known or suspected cancer case, also attempt to image Level VII (superior mediastinum) by angling the probe inferiorly at the sternal notch.

5. **When cancer is confirmed or strongly suspected**: map suspicious nodes level by level, measure short and long axes, describe morphology, and identify the best target for aspiration — usually the most accessible node with the most suspicious features, not necessarily the largest.

## Thyroid Malignancy: Subtypes and Their Sonographic Appearances

Each histological subtype has a characteristic biological behaviour that is reflected sonographically. Knowing the "why" helps you recognise atypical presentations.

### Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)

PTC is the most common thyroid malignancy and the one you will encounter most frequently. It tends to be solid, markedly hypoechoic, with microcalcifications (psammoma bodies), irregular or lobulated margins, and a taller-than-wide shape in transverse section. The combination of these features with suspicious lateral neck nodes — often cystic, sometimes containing microcalcifications — is practically diagnostic.

Important variants to know: the **tall-cell variant** is more aggressive, often markedly hypoechoic with dense microcalcifications. The **diffuse sclerosing variant** can mimic Hashimoto's thyroiditis, with scattered microcalcifications throughout a heterogeneous gland rather than a discrete nodule — this is easy to miss if you are not looking for it.

### Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

FTC presents a genuine diagnostic challenge because it is often indistinguishable from a benign follicular adenoma: solid, iso- or hypoechoic, smooth margin, peripheral halo, sometimes with peripheral vascularity. Capsular and vascular invasion — the criteria that define malignancy — cannot be assessed by ultrasound or cytology; they require histology. For this reason, a Thy3f result on FNA triggers surgical lobectomy rather than simple observation. FTC spreads predominantly haematogenously (lung, bone), so cervical nodal disease is uncommon.

### Hurthle Cell (Oncocytic) Carcinoma

Hurthle cell lesions are frequently solid, hypoechoic or heterogeneous with coarse calcifications, and often hypervascular. They are typically classified as Thy3f on cytology, and histological assessment of the resected specimen is required.

### Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)

MTC arises from parafollicular C cells and so does not produce thyroglobulin. Sonographically it appears as a solid hypoechoic nodule with punctate or coarse calcifications and prominent internal vascularity. Multifocality is common in familial/MEN2 disease. Nodal metastases occur early, often bilaterally, and the nodes may calcify. The key correlate here is serum **calcitonin and CEA**, not Tg. Any suspicious node in an MTC patient should have **calcitonin washout**, not Tg washout, at the time of FNA.

### Anaplastic and Poorly Differentiated Carcinoma

These are the most aggressive thyroid malignancies. Sonographically you will see a large, heterogeneous, ill-defined mass with areas of necrosis, possibly gas if there is tumour-tracheal communication, and evidence of vascular encasement. Nodes are bulky, necrotic, and often bilateral. A history of prior differentiated thyroid cancer may be present. Core biopsy is usually required for definitive diagnosis. These patients need urgent cross-sectional imaging and should be discussed at MDT without delay.

### Thyroid Lymphoma

Lymphoma typically presents as diffuse hypoechoic enlargement — sometimes with a "pseudoglandular" reticulated pattern — or as a lobulated hypoechoic mass against a background of Hashimoto's thyroiditis. Vascularity is markedly increased. Because lymphoma requires immunophenotyping for classification, **core biopsy is preferred** over FNA.

## The U1–U5 Thyroid Nodule System: Principles and Practice

The U classification is not a scoring system to apply mechanically — it is a structured way of communicating risk based on features that reflect tumour biology. Understanding *why* each feature is worrying helps you apply the system confidently and handle edge cases with judgement.

| U Category | Interpretation | Typical Features |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **U1** | Normal | No focal lesion |
| **U2** | Benign | Spongiform; cystic with comet-tail artefacts; isoechoic with halo; smooth margins; intact eggshell calcification |
| **U3** | Indeterminate | Solid hyperechoic/isoechoic; mixed solid/cystic without definitive benign signs; equivocal halo; mild lobulation |
| **U4** | Suspicious | Solid hypoechoic; irregular or lobulated margins; possible microcalcifications |
| **U5** | Highly suspicious/malignant | Markedly hypoechoic with ≥1 of: microcalcifications, taller-than-wide, spiculated margins, ETE, or pathognomonic suspicious nodes |

A few principles are worth emphasising:

**Echogenicity** is informative because malignant thyroid tissue is densely cellular with little colloid, reducing acoustic backscatter. *Marked* hypoechogenicity (darker than strap muscle) is a more specific sign than mild hypoechogenicity alone.

**Microcalcifications** are psammoma bodies — do not confuse them with colloid artefacts. Colloid produces comet-tail artefacts that fan out posteriorly; microcalcifications produce discreet posterior acoustic shadowing. Getting this distinction right is one of the most practically important things you can do at the console.

**Taller-than-wide shape** reflects growth perpendicular to normal tissue planes — tumour expanding against, rather than constrained by, follicular architecture.

**Spongiform composition** (≥50% microcystic) and **comet-tail artefacts** are reassuringly benign, reflecting colloid-filled follicles.

**Disrupted eggshell calcification** is a specific concern: an intact peripheral rim of calcification is a U2 feature, but if there is focal interruption with protruding soft tissue, upgrade to at least U4.

### Biopsy Thresholds

These vary by centre and are updated periodically — always follow your local guidance. As a general framework:

- **U5**: FNA at any size, or if very small (e.g., <5 mm) and the primary is clearly established, the MDT may defer and rely on nodal sampling.
- **U4**: FNA if ≥10 mm; consider earlier if ETE is suspected or nodes are suspicious.
- **U3**: FNA if ≥15 mm, or if growing on surveillance imaging, or in a high-risk clinical context.
- **U2**: FNA not usually indicated unless ≥20 mm with growth or symptoms, or in a high-risk patient (prior head and neck radiation, familial cancer syndrome).
- Suspicious lymph nodes should be sampled **regardless of primary nodule size**.
- Children and young adults warrant a lower threshold throughout.

The point of the U system is not to replace clinical judgement but to provide a reproducible framework for MDT decision-making. A U3 nodule in a patient with a family history of MEN2 and a raised calcitonin is not managed the same way as an isolated U3 finding in a 60-year-old with no risk factors.

## Cytology, Core Biopsy, and Biochemical Correlation

### The Thy Cytology Categories

The RCPath Thy classification maps to management as follows:

- **Thy1/Thy1c** (non-diagnostic, including cystic): repeat FNA, ideally with on-site adequacy assessment. Targeting the solid component or a septal wall rather than fluid will improve diagnostic yield.
- **Thy2** (benign): discharge or surveillance depending on U score and clinical context.
- **Thy3a** (atypia of undetermined significance): repeat FNA; individualise management based on U score, interval growth, and clinical risk.
- **Thy3f** (follicular neoplasm or Hürthle cell lesion): lobectomy is generally recommended, as capsular/vascular invasion cannot be excluded on cytology alone.
- **Thy4** (suspicious of malignancy): treat as cancer; proceed to staging.
- **Thy5** (malignant): confirmed; proceed to definitive management.

### Core Biopsy

Core biopsy is appropriate for suspected lymphoma (requiring immunophenotyping), anaplastic carcinoma, and repeatedly non-diagnostic FNA from a suspicious lesion. The bleeding risk is small but real — ensure coagulation is satisfactory and that a post-biopsy compression plan is in place.

### Biochemical Integration

**Thyroglobulin (Tg)**: not useful for pre-operative diagnosis of a nodule, but central to post-operative DTC surveillance. When performing FNA of a node suspicious for DTC, send a separate saline rinse for **Tg washout**. Any detectable Tg in the washout fluid — particularly if higher than the concurrent serum Tg — is highly suspicious for metastatic DTC.
