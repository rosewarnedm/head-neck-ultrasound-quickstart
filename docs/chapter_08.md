# Chapter 8: The Neck Lump — Congenital and Cystic Lesions

---

## Why This Chapter Matters

Cystic neck lumps arrive on your list from three main sources: GP two-week-wait (2WW) referrals, ENT outpatient clinics, and head and neck oncology MDTs. In children, the vast majority are congenital and benign. In adults — particularly with a lateral neck mass — the same cystic sonographic appearance can represent metastatic squamous cell carcinoma from an HPV-positive oropharyngeal primary (OPSCC), or a cystic nodal deposit from papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC). Getting this distinction wrong in either direction has real consequences: missing a cancer on a 2WW pathway, or unnecessarily alarming a patient with a simple thyroglossal duct cyst.

Your job is to read the anatomy, integrate age and location, interrogate the internal architecture with Doppler, and direct the next step — whether that is reassurance and discharge, elective ENT referral, image-guided FNA with ancillary tests, or same-day escalation onto the 2WW cancer pathway.

## Practical Foundations

### Probe Selection and Setup

Use a high-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz) for most neck lesions. Switch to a 6–9 MHz curvilinear for deep or bulky masses. A small-footprint "hockey-stick" probe is useful for the floor of mouth and in paediatric patients where the acoustic window is tight.

When scanning a potential cyst, your gain and dynamic range settings matter more than people realise:

- **Keep gain low enough** that genuine fluid is truly anechoic. If you over-gain, you risk adding pseudo-echoes that make a benign cyst appear suspicious, or conversely, masking real debris.
- **Widen dynamic range** to separate thin walls from surrounding fat.
- **Colour and power Doppler**: reduce PRF to detect slow septal or mural flow. For venous malformations, reduce PRF further still and increase colour persistence — you need very low wall filters to pick up sluggish venous flow. Getting this wrong makes a vascular malformation look completely avascular, which will mislead you.

### Patient Positioning and Dynamic Manoeuvres

Position the patient supine with a small shoulder roll and the head rotated away from the side of interest. Several dynamic tests add diagnostic information that a static scan cannot:

- **Swallowing and tongue protrusion** for midline or paramedian lesions — a thyroglossal duct cyst (TDC) moves cranially with tongue protrusion because of its attachment to the foramen caecum via the thyroglossal tract.
- **Valsalva** expands venous malformations and laryngoceles.
- **Bidigital palpation** helps assess ranulas and floor of mouth lesions.
- **Gentle compression** demonstrates deformability and septal mobility in lymphatic malformations; avoid vigorous compression over a suspected abscess.

Acquire cine loops rather than static images for dynamic tests — subtle movement is easily missed on a freeze-frame.

### Systematic Cervical Sweep and Anatomy

Always sweep the entire neck bilaterally before focusing on the palpable lesion. A single-node biopsy targeted without context misses concomitant disease that would change management. Localise every lesion to cervical levels I–VI:

- **Level I**: submental (IA) and submandibular (IB) — bounded by the anterior belly of digastric.
- **Levels II–IV**: along the internal jugular chain, from the skull base to the clavicle.
- **Level V**: posterior triangle.
- **Level VI**: central compartment — pre- and paratracheal, from hyoid to sternum.

Within each level, note relationships to key landmarks: the hyoid body and thyrohyoid membrane; the mylohyoid muscle and its natural dehiscences; the sternocleidomastoid (SCM) border; the carotid sheath; the parotid tail; and the thyroid gland. These relationships are the anatomical clues that distinguish most congenital cysts from one another.

### What Makes a Cyst Look Like a Cyst — and When It Doesn't

Pure fluid is anechoic, transmits sound well (posterior acoustic enhancement), and has a sharply defined back wall. Any departure from this deserves explanation:

- **Internal echoes** arise from protein, mucin, cholesterol crystals, or haemorrhage. Low-level homogeneous debris is reassuring if the rest of the architecture is benign. Heterogeneous or layering debris is less so.
- **Septations** represent fibrous bands, lymphatic walls, or granulation tissue. Thin, avascular septa in a multilocular cyst are typical of lymphatic malformation. Thick, vascular septa raise the differential of a complex neoplastic process.
- **Mural nodules** are the critical feature. A true benign cyst has no vascular solid component. Demonstrable internal vascularity within a mural nodule is suspicious for neoplasia until proven otherwise — do not dismiss this finding.

The key Doppler principle: a true cyst has no intrinsic internal vascularity. Peripheral or septal flow can occur with inflammation. Nodular internal flow mandates oncological consideration in an adult.

### Reporting Language and Recommendations

A useful report for a cystic neck lump includes:

- Size in three planes; depth from skin; precise level and anatomical space.
- Internal architecture: anechoic vs debris; septal number and thickness; mural nodules; wall thickness; calcification; posterior enhancement.
- Doppler findings: explicitly state whether flow is present in the wall, septa, or any solid component.
- Dynamic behaviour when tested.
- Findings from the whole-neck survey: additional nodes, thyroid assessment with U-score, salivary glands, contralateral comparison.
- A clear recommendation: reassure and discharge / elective ENT referral / antibiotics and review / image-guided FNA or core biopsy / 2WW activation.

Vague reports that describe appearances without a recommendation are not useful in a neck lump clinic.

## Age, Location, and Oncological Context

This is the single most important conceptual framework in the chapter. Before you pick up the probe, ask yourself: how old is this patient, and where is the lump?

- **Children and adolescents**: congenital lesions dominate — lymphatic malformation, TDC, dermoid/epidermoid, ranula, thymic remnants. Cystic nodal metastasis is rare.
- **Adults with a midline or paramedian suprahyoid cyst**: more likely benign (TDC, dermoid), though atypical features warrant sampling.
- **Adults with a lateral neck cystic mass, particularly at level II**: this is metastatic disease until proven otherwise. In UK practice, this should trigger a 2WW suspected head and neck cancer referral and coordinated FNA with HPV testing.

The age threshold is not absolute — a 30-year-old can have a true branchial cleft cyst — but you need an explicit reason *not* to escalate a new lateral cystic neck mass in an adult, not an explicit reason *to* escalate. Default to caution.

## Lesion-by-Lesion: Appearance, Anatomy, Pitfalls, and Pathway

### 1. Thyroglossal Duct Cyst

**Why it is where it is**: the thyroid gland descends from the foramen caecum to its final position, pulling the thyroglossal tract with it. The tract passes through or around the hyoid body, which is why TDCs tend to abut or indent the hyoid. Most are midline or paramedian; the majority are infrahyoid, though suprahyoid and intralingual variants occur.

**Ultrasound**: an uncomplicated TDC is a well-circumscribed, thinly walled, anechoic cyst with posterior enhancement, typically embedded in or immediately adjacent to the strap muscles near the hyoid. Infected or previously inflamed cysts develop internal echoes, fluid–fluid levels, and a thickened hyperaemic wall. Some contain echogenic, vascular tissue within the wall — this represents ectopic thyroid tissue and is a normal variant, but it must be distinguished from the rare but important papillary carcinoma arising within the cyst wall.

The **dynamic sign** is diagnostically useful: watch in cine as the patient protrudes their tongue — a TDC will move cranially. This is not pathognomonic but supports the diagnosis in the right context.

**Key pitfalls**:
- Always confirm a normal orthotopic thyroid before planning surgery. If the TDC contains the only functional thyroid tissue (rare but real), inadvertent excision is catastrophic.
- A solid mural nodule with internal vascularity in an adult should prompt FNA of that component and cross-sectional imaging to exclude malignancy. Papillary carcinoma arising within a TDC is uncommon but well recognised.
- Infrahyoid TDCs can be confused with exophytic thyroid nodules or laryngeal lesions — confirm the relationship to the thyroid and check for a normal isthmus.

**Pathway**: typical uncomplicated TDC in a child requires elective ENT review; US alone suffices. Atypical features or solid nodules in an adult → targeted FNA, apply thyroid U-classification to any thyroid nodule, consider CT or MRI.

### 2. Branchial Cleft Anomalies

**Embryology and location**: the second branchial apparatus gives rise to the vast majority of clinically encountered branchial anomalies. The classic site is level II — anteromedial to the SCM, lateral to the carotid sheath, often described as lying along a line from the tonsillar fossa to the mid-neck. They may present as a cyst alone, a sinus (external opening on the skin), a fistula (connecting oropharynx to skin), or an asymptomatic tract.

First cleft anomalies are less common and deserve separate mention: they arise near the parotid or external auditory canal and may lie adjacent to or traverse the facial nerve — this has obvious surgical implications.

Third and fourth cleft/pouch anomalies are uncommon and tend to present with recurrent left-sided suppurative thyroiditis via a pyriform sinus fistula. If you encounter recurrent left thyroid lobe infections in a child, think of this diagnosis.

**Ultrasound**: a well-defined, thin-walled anechoic cyst at the characteristic level II site is the typical appearance. Infected cysts develop internal debris and wall thickening.

**The critical adult pitfall — this cannot be overstated**: in an adult, an apparently simple lateral cystic neck mass at level II should not be labelled "branchial cleft cyst" without carefully excluding a cystic nodal metastasis from HPV-positive OPSCC. The two can be sonographically indistinguishable. Features that should raise your suspicion for metastasis include:

- Age over 30–40 (though HPV-related disease can present younger)
- Thick or irregular walls, eccentric mural nodules with internal vascularity
- Vascular internal septa
- Calcification (pointing more towards thyroid origin)
- Multiple or conglomerate nodes in the same chain
- Any additional abnormal nodes elsewhere in the neck

If even one of these features is present, escalate. Even if none are present but the patient is an adult, maintain a low threshold for FNA and 2WW referral. The diagnosis of "benign second branchial cleft cyst" should be one of exclusion in an adult.

**Pathway**: adult with new lateral cystic mass → 2WW, FNA targeting any solid mural component with HPV testing (p16 immunocytochemistry or HPV DNA/RNA in cytology fluid), contrast-enhanced CT or MRI for staging and primary site assessment, PET-CT if primary remains occult after endoscopy. In children with classic second cleft anomalies, elective ENT suffices.

### 3. Lymphatic Malformations

**Behaviour**: these are abnormal lymphatic channels present from birth, typically enlarging over time or with intercurrent infection or haemorrhage. They are classified as macrocystic (locules >1–2 cm), microcystic, or mixed. Crucially, they are infiltrative — they insinuate between tissue planes rather than displacing them, which means they can be trans-spatial and may extend into the mediastinum or axilla.

**Ultrasound**: the classic appearance is a multiloculated, compressible cystic mass with thin avascular septa, usually in the posterior triangle or lower face/neck. The septa may wobble with gentle compression. Fluid–fluid levels indicate prior haemorrhage into a locule. There is no solid vascular internal component.

**Differentials**:
- **Venous malformation**: look for phleboliths (echogenic foci with posterior shadowing — essentially pathognomonic), compressibility with slow internal venous flow on very low PRF, and augmentation on Valsalva. Lymphatic malformations lack phleboliths and intralesional venous flow.
- **Abscess**: thick hyperaemic wall, uniform internal debris, pain, systemic sepsis, sometimes gas artefact.
- **Ranula**: confined to floor of mouth/submandibular space with a mylohyoid tail — not trans-spatial.

**Pathway**: US to screen; MRI to map trans-spatial extent before any intervention. Management is MDT-based — macrocystic disease responds well to percutaneous sclerotherapy; microcystic disease is much more challenging surgically and interventionally.

### 4. Dermoid and Epidermoid Cysts

**Anatomy**: these arise from ectodermal inclusions along embryological fusion lines, most commonly in the midline submental region. The relationship to mylohyoid is surgically important: lesions above mylohyoid present as floor of mouth swelling; those below present as submental or submandibular swelling.

**Ultrasound**: dermoids are recognisable by their internal content — multiple echogenic floating globules with posterior acoustic enhancement ("sack of marbles" sign), representing fat lobules or keratin debris. They are avascular internally. Epidermoids tend to show homogeneous low-level echoes or a laminated appearance, closer to a simple cyst.

**Differentials**:
- TDC: deeper, more posterior, near the hyoid, moves with tongue protrusion.
- Ranula: lateralised, in the sublingual space, with mylohyoid tail.
- Lipoma: linear echogenic striations, very compressible, follows fascial planes.

**Pathway**: elective surgical excision. Ultrasound defines the mylohyoid relationship, which guides the operative approach. MRI for large, deep, or diagnostically uncertain lesions.

### 5. Ranula — Simple and Plunging

**Anatomy**: a ranula is a mucus extravasation pseudocyst arising from the sublingual gland. A *simple ranula* is confined above the mylohyoid in the sublingual space; a *plunging ranula* herniates through a dehiscence in mylohyoid into the submandibular space and neck.

**Ultrasound**: the simple ranula appears as a thin-walled anechoic cyst in the floor of mouth alongside the sublingual gland — use a small probe with gel standoff to avoid near-field artefact. The plunging ranula presents as a cystic neck mass with a visible tail tracking superiorly through a mylohyoid defect. The "tail sign" is the key to diagnosis: look for it deliberately by scanning from the neck upward through the mylohyoid. There is no internal vascularity.

**Pitfalls**:
- Focusing only on the neck component and missing the mylohyoid tail — always trace the full extent.
- Confusing a plunging ranula with a second branchial cyst (ranulas are in the submandibular space, tethered to the sublingual gland; second branchial cysts are along the anterior SCM at level II and deeper).
- Dilated Wharton's duct with a stone is a different diagnosis — look for an echogenic calculus with posterior shadowing.

**Pathway**: ENT or oral surgery referral. MRI is useful when the US window is restricted or when the mylohyoid tail is not confidently seen.
