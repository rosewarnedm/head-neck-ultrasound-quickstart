# Chapter 7: Cervical Lymphadenopathy

Cervical lymph nodes are the most common neck lump referred for ultrasound in day-to-day practice — from GP two-week-wait (2WW) referrals and ENT outpatient clinics to head and neck oncology staging and post-treatment surveillance. Ultrasound earns its place here because it resolves nodal microanatomy in real time, drives immediate image-guided sampling, and avoids ionising radiation. The goal of this chapter is not to give you a list of features to memorise, but to help you understand *why* a node looks the way it does — so that when you encounter an atypical case, you can reason from first principles rather than pattern-match and miss.

---

## Sonographic Anatomy and Lymphatic Pathways

### Why Nodes Look the Way They Do

Understanding three anatomical layers tells you almost everything:

**Cortex.** A peripheral zone of tightly packed lymphocytes. These scatter sound poorly relative to adjacent fat, so the cortex appears as a thin, hypoechoic rim on ultrasound. In reactive hyperplasia, lymphocyte proliferation thickens this rim but preserves its uniform, circumferential architecture. In metastatic disease, tumour deposits arrive via afferent lymphatics and seed the subcapsular sinus first — which is why early metastasis produces *focal or eccentric* cortical thickening rather than uniform enlargement. As disease progresses, the cortex expands globally and the next layer is consumed.

**Medulla and hilum.** Central fatty stroma through which medullary sinuses and the nodal vessels run. The fat creates the echogenic central stripe you recognise as the hilum. Hilar vessels — afferent and efferent lymphatics plus the hilar artery and vein — enter and exit at this gateway, producing the characteristic branching, low-resistance Doppler signal. When cortical tumour expands inward, it compresses and eventually effaces the hilum. Simultaneously, tumour-induced angiogenesis generates disorganised new vessels at the periphery, shifting the Doppler pattern from central/hilar to peripheral or mixed. This is the vascular correlate of malignancy, and understanding it makes the Doppler criterion logical rather than arbitrary.

**Capsule and perinodal fat.** A thin fibrous capsule separates the node from adjacent echogenic fat. Normally this interface is crisp. Inflammation increases perinodal vascularity and oedema, making the fat appear echogenic and slightly stranded. Extranodal extension (ENE) of tumour breaches the capsule, producing irregular or spiculated margins, obliterated fat planes, and fixation to adjacent muscle or vessels — findings you must actively seek and report, because ENE significantly worsens prognosis and alters surgical planning.

These principles unify the grey-scale and Doppler features discussed throughout this chapter.

### Cervical Nodal Levels (I–VI)

The level system is the shared language of head and neck oncology. Knowing it lets you communicate with surgeons and oncologists precisely and lets you predict the likely primary when you find an abnormal node.

| Level | Boundaries / key landmarks | Key draining sites |
|---|---|---|
| IA (submental) | Between anterior digastric bellies, below symphysis menti | Lip, anterior floor of mouth, chin |
| IB (submandibular) | Submandibular triangle, around the gland | Oral cavity, anterior face |
| IIA / IIB (upper jugular) | Skull base to hyoid; IIA anterior/inferior, IIB posterior to CN XI | Oropharynx, nasopharynx, oral cavity, larynx |
| III (mid-jugular) | Hyoid to cricoid | Oropharynx, hypopharynx, larynx |
| IV (lower jugular) | Cricoid to clavicle | Hypopharynx, larynx, thyroid; Virchow node (left) |
| V (posterior triangle) | Between SCM and trapezius | Nasopharynx, scalp, posterior neck |
| VI (central compartment) | Pretracheal, paratracheal, Delphian | Thyroid, larynx, upper oesophagus |

A few clinical correlations you will use at the machine every day:

- **HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC** classically presents as a cystic or partly cystic level II or III node in a middle-aged adult who may have minimal primary-site symptoms. This is now one of the most frequent referral patterns in UK 2WW clinics.
- **Thyroid carcinoma** (particularly papillary) spreads first to level VI, then laterally to II–IV. Isolated lateral metastases without level VI involvement do occur ("skip metastases").
- **Scalp, face, and auricular skin malignancies** drain to intraparotid and preauricular nodes and to the upper jugular chain. A small intraparotid node without a hilum in a patient with facial skin SCC or melanoma is a high-stakes finding.
- **Left supraclavicular (Virchow) nodes** should prompt cross-sectional imaging of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis when they appear morphologically suspicious, because they may represent the first sign of a thoracic or abdominal primary.

Level VII (superior mediastinal) is largely beyond routine ultrasound reach but can be partially visualised in lean patients using a curvilinear probe in the suprasternal notch.

## Equipment, Patient Positioning, and Scanning Technique

### Probe Choice and Settings

Use a **high-frequency linear probe (12–18 MHz)** for most cervical nodes. Drop to **9–12 MHz** for deep or posteriorly situated nodes in larger necks. A **hockey-stick probe** (small footprint, high frequency) is invaluable for the submental region and in paediatric patients. For the lower central compartment and supraclavicular fossa, a **5–9 MHz curvilinear probe** supplements the linear.

Apply a **small-parts preset** optimised for high spatial resolution. For Doppler, begin with colour or power Doppler at a **low pulse repetition frequency (PRF) of 0.5–1.0 kHz**, low wall filter, and gain turned up to just below noise. Sweep slowly. These settings detect slow hilar flow — which is physiological — and prevent you from falsely labelling a reactive node as avascular, a common and consequential error.

Practical points worth emphasising:

- **Probe pressure is your enemy in Doppler.** Even moderate compression collapses low-flow hilar vessels. If you see no Doppler signal, reduce pressure first before concluding a node is avascular.
- **Peripheral colour flicker from probe motion** can mimic capsular neovascularity. Freeze and switch to power Doppler, which is less directional and slightly more motion-resistant, to confirm.
- Spectral Doppler resistive indices are inconsistently discriminatory for malignancy; do not over-rely on them for clinical decisions.
- **Elastography** is offered on many modern machines. Malignant nodes tend to be stiffer than muscle, whilst necrotic nodes may be paradoxically soft. The technique is an adjunct at best — cut-offs vary between systems, and it should not override grey-scale and Doppler assessment.

### Patient Positioning

Supine with a pillow under the shoulders to extend the neck; head turned away from the side being scanned. For the submental region, minimal extension or slight flexion with the hockey-stick probe works better. Exposing the neck fully and using warm gel improves patient comfort and image quality.

### A Reproducible, Level-Based Sweep

**Always scan both sides**. The most suspicious node may not be the palpable lump, and skip lesions and contralateral disease are clinically important.

1. **Level IA (submental):** Begin at the mandibular symphysis with transverse sweeps, using the anterior digastric bellies as landmarks, and slide to the hyoid.
2. **Level IB (submandibular):** Track along the submandibular gland; distinguish intraglandular nodes (which have a hilum) from gland parenchyma.
3. **Parotid and preauricular:** Scan within the parotid in transverse and longitudinal planes, looking for intraparotid nodes. This level is easily overlooked but critical in skin malignancy pathways.
4. **Level II (upper jugular):** Identify the internal jugular vein (IJV) and carotid artery; angle the probe posteriorly and cranially toward the skull base. IIB nodes lurk posterior to the SCM — rotate and angulate to find them. Attempting to see CN XI is ideal but not always feasible.
5. **Levels III and IV (mid and lower jugular):** Slide caudally along the carotid sheath to the clavicle, maintaining both the vessel landmarks and the nodal tissue in view.
6. **Level V (posterior triangle):** From the mastoid tip to the clavicle between SCM and trapezius; include the supraclavicular fossa, angling medially.
7. **Level VI (central compartment):** Parasagittal sweeps along the trachea, adjacent to and between the thyroid lobes, down to the sternal notch. Record the Delphian (prelaryngeal) node.

At each level, document the largest node and the morphologically most suspicious node — these may not be the same.

## The Normal Lymph Node

Knowing what normal looks like with precision is the foundation for recognising abnormality:

- **Shape:** Oval, with a long-axis to short-axis ratio greater than 2.
- **Size:** Short-axis typically 3–7 mm at most levels; level II nodes are physiologically larger (up to 10–11 mm short axis is often acceptable in context).
- **Cortex:** Thin (generally <3 mm), uniform, hypoechoic rim.
- **Hilum:** Central echogenic stripe with branching vessels on Doppler.
- **Echotexture:** Homogeneous, no focal bulges or necrosis.
- **Vascularity:** Hilar branching pattern only; low resistance; no peripheral capsular flow.
- **Margins:** Smooth; crisp interface with perinodal fat.
- **Mobility:** Glides over adjacent structures on probe compression.

**Key teaching point:** A preserved hilum with purely central/hilar Doppler flow is more reassuring than small size alone. Conversely, the absence of a hilum in a small node is not automatically benign — context matters.

## Recognising Abnormality: The Principles

Malignant replacement begins at the cortex and works inward. Keeping this in mind lets you interpret each feature mechanistically:

| Stage | Appearances |
|---|---|
| Early malignancy | Focal or eccentric cortical bulge, hilum preserved elsewhere |
| Established malignancy | Global cortical thickening, hilum effaced, shape increasingly round, peripheral/mixed Doppler |
| Advanced malignancy | Intranodal necrosis, calcification (tumour-type dependent), ENE features |

**Inflammation** produces different kinetics: cortical thickening is diffuse and symmetric; brisk hilar vascularity (not peripheral); perinodal fat echogenicity from oedema; and in suppuration, central liquefaction with marked peripheral hyperaemia.

**Lymphoma** replaces nodal parenchyma diffusely without the stepwise cortical invasion seen in metastases. Nodes become homogeneously and profoundly hypoechoic, often with prominent central vascularity and without early necrosis or calcification. The multilevel, bilateral distribution is the critical contextual clue.

These are patterns, not rules. Individual nodes in inflammatory disease can look suspicious; individual early metastatic nodes can look reassuringly oval. Accumulate all available features and exercise judgement.

## Grey-Scale and Doppler Criteria in Detail

### Features That Increase Suspicion

**Shape.** A round node (long/short ratio <2) reflects confluent cortical replacement. Be cautious: level IIB nodes are often naturally rounder, and recent tonsillitis can produce plump level II nodes in young adults. Shape is contributory, not determinative.

**Cortical assessment.** Focal or eccentric cortical thickening exceeding 3 mm, or a discrete nodular cortical bulge, is more specific for metastasis than global thickening. Global but symmetric cortical thickening with a preserved hilum in a tender group of nodes is much more likely to represent reactive change.

**Hilum.** Effacement or displacement of the central echogenic stripe favours malignancy or lymphoma. However, a preserved hilum does not exclude early metastasis or early lymphoma — it is reassuring but not exculpatory.

**Margins.** Irregular, lobulated, or angulated contours raise concern. Smooth, well-defined margins are associated with benignity but are also seen in thyroid and lymphomatous deposits.

**Echotexture.** Three specific patterns deserve emphasis:

- *Cystic or necrotic foci* within an otherwise solid node strongly favour malignancy in an adult, particularly HPV-positive oropharyngeal SCC or papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) metastases. Suppurative lymphadenitis is the main infective mimic and is usually clinically obvious.
- *Microcalcifications* — punctate echogenic foci with posterior acoustic shadow or colour Doppler twinkling artefact — are hallmarks of PTC nodal metastases. Coarse calcifications suggest either chronic granulomatous disease or previously treated nodes.
- *Homogeneous hypoechogenicity* without necrosis in multiple enlarged bilateral nodes is the classic lymphoma pattern.

**Vascularity.** With deliberately light probe pressure: hilar-only branching flow is reassuring; peripheral or mixed flow with capsular vessels suggests tumour-induced neovascularisation. An avascular node with solid components is suspicious for necrotic metastasis or liquefied abscess — the wall and any solid component should be sampled rather than the acellular contents.

**Perinodal fat.** Echogenic stranding, obscured fat planes, or a hazy perinodal "halo" may be the earliest sign of ENE or surrounding inflammation. In a known or suspected malignancy context, report any abnormal perinodal fat and recommend cross-sectional imaging for staging, because ENE materially changes surgical and oncological management.

## Common Pathologies and Their Differentials

### Reactive and Infectious Lymphadenopathy

This is the most common finding in GP referrals. Typical appearances: oval, moderately enlarged nodes in groups; uniform cortical thickening; preserved echogenic hilum; brisk hilar Doppler; possibly echogenic perinodal fat in the acute phase. Clinical tenderness and a plausible infective cause should make you confident in a reactive diagnosis, provided no suspicious morphological features are present. Always document that the criteria for malignancy are absent, not merely that nodes "are present."

**Suppurative lymphadenitis** adds central hypoechogenicity with posterior enhancement, internal debris, and intense peripheral hyperaemia. Gas inclusions (echogenic foci with ring-down artefact) indicate gas-forming organisms. Ultrasound-guided aspiration is often the first-line therapeutic intervention, particularly in children, both for diagnosis and drainage.

**Tuberculosis and atypical mycobacteria** deserve specific mention because they are not rare in UK practice and have characteristic features: matted, closely apposed nodes; central caseous necrosis producing heterogeneous hypoechoic cores ("cold abscess"); sinus tract formation in chronic disease; coarse calcification; and peripheral Doppler flow around avascular necrotic centres. If TB is in the differential, send aspirate in sterile saline (not formalin) for AFB smear, culture, and PCR (including GeneXpert). A core biopsy for histology may be needed to demonstrate granulomata.

**Viral adenopathy** (EBV, CMV, HIV) tends to produce bilateral, multilevel, relatively preserved-architecture nodes. Check for hepatosplenomegaly. HIV-related lymphadenopathy can coexist with opportunistic infections or lymphoma; a low threshold for sampling is appropriate if the clinical picture is complex.

In **paediatric practice**, larger reactive nodes are entirely normal; size thresholds are less reliable, and morphological criteria should be applied with greater conservatism. Clinical follow-up rather than immediate biopsy is appropriate unless there are red flags (persistent >6 weeks despite antibiotics, systemic symptoms, or morphological malignancy features).

### Granulomatous and Systemic Disease

Sarcoidosis, IgG4-related disease, and Kikuchi–Fujimoto disease can all produce enlarged hypoechoic nodes that are morphologically difficult to distinguish from lymphoma on ultrasound alone. Clinical context (age, systemic features, associated gland enlargement in IgG4) guides prioritisation, but tissue diagnosis is usually required.
